The baker was kneading the dough. Folding it on itself, working and punching it with his fists, over and over again. He could have used a mixer for that - the small one was empty - but he always did the top-shelf items himself, start to finish.

The power had come back yesterday, the day after they arrived. He didn't know why, but he had a feeling it would stay on this time, as if all the ingredients had finally been measured correctly. That evening, he had switched on the oven to let the heat build. That morning, he had got up before sunrise, to prepare the  largest batch of bread-dough his biggest mixer could fit, leaving it to rise. It was a risk - if the power went out again, he would have wasted most of his remaining flour.

***

During the first hour, two men in mill overalls came and went, the tags hanging from their necks frosted in the morning chill. The second hour, a group of three men and a woman, in patched clothes and with unwashed hair, and no hurry to go anywhere. They had bought a roll of bread and a coffee. Now, it was the third hour, and the baker had already lost count of the customers. He had even sold two of the crispy sickles from the top shelf. Normally, only the administrators and other merchants bought them, late afternoon.

The penny-rolls were still the best-sellers - small, simple breads with a hard crust. They sold for a few pennies - chips of the local currency, the color of burnt bread, thin and frayed. 

Sometimes, they sold for something else entirely: yesterday's magazine, a sweeping of the floors, or a favour owned. Once, a girl had offered to sweep his bedroom instead, but he had sent her away with a bag of stale rolls and some dry crackers. She wouldn't have smelled of flour, and would have left a bad taste in his mouth, too.

Even Lady Threepeak came. He had not seen her in weeks and had begun to think her time had finally come. She paid with the copper coins that had been out of circulation since he was a boy, and a cracked button besides.

***

The moon was high when he finally locked the door. It was later than usual. The baker didn't remember when it had last been so late.

Maybe the caravans would even start coming again. He was running out of flour, and the local stuff he could buy had chips of bran in it and didn't rise as well as he liked. He only had local coin, but maybe he could sweet-talk the head itinerant to agree to an exchange. His signature cake for a bag or two perhaps. Failing that, he could always penny-roll his way to a bottle of the local moonshine, and then palm it off on a caravan loader.

The baker switched off the power to the oven. The massive mound of brick, dusted with flour, was thoroughly heated by now. Tomorrow morning, it would be ready to use with only an hour or two of heating.

As long as the power stayed on, the rolls would keep coming. And as long as the rolls came, people would too. 

As long as the power stayed on.

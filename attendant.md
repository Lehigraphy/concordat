The numbers were off.

He had checked the books from previous years three times now, to see if he had misread a column somewhere. He had redone the calculations for this year, counting the beads until the moon rose. He had even recalculated some of the old numbers, to see if he had made a mistake then.

Everything was correct, but he simply couldn’t shake the feeling.

There were too many children in uptown.

That was great, of course. He had had many children himself, and seven had reached adulthood.

But there were _more_ than there should be.

That was peculiar. Even the mountain devils of the old faith just took children, never brought more.

Counting was his work, and he was good at it. Counting citizens meant knowing how long the granaries would last, and what could be achieved with next year’s taxes. Counting children meant knowing how many apprentices the foundry or the mill would have in ten years time, or how many lesson-givers were needed in five.

Now, he didn’t know anymore.

***

He was sitting in his room, going over his notes, a jug of ale untouched on the table.

He had walked to uptown and paid the innkeeper for a week. The streets were narrow, and waste water trickled in ditches near the brick and wood walls. Kids were running around. He knew he had passed close to a foundry from the thin layer of soot covering everything. A goat came to push its head against him near the well. He scratched it behind the ear and gave it a piece of bread he’d been saving for himself. The animal bleated happily and ran off. The people standing in line for water smiled.

Earlier that day he had met with the district record keeper. They had more detailed books than he had available at the prime hall. They had gone through them all, checked their numbers against his.

The birth counts matched. The census counts matched.

All the uptown numbers matched what he had.

What had caught his attention was between the rows.

More babies were living than he had accounted for.

_Many_ more babies were living around the uptown foundry. Elsewhere, the counts were as he had come to expect.

He took a long sip of his ale. It had gone stale, but he suddenly realized he was thirsty.

He would go through other books tomorrow. Walk through the storerooms and eateries. He suspected there was something in what the people were eating, something that gave birth to robust babies.

He had also considered the smoke, for a while. The air was clearly more acrid here, stinging his eyes. Smoke stuck to walls and often made old foundry workers cough up black phlegm. That did not look like health.

***

Fevers.

Another night, another jug of ale. New notes.

Very few babies were lost to fevers around the foundry.

Fewer mothers, too. He had missed that earlier. The difference was small, but it was clear enough once he knew to look for it.

Last year, a trader had sailed in with a book on comparing numbers properly, not just counting them. It had been hideously expensive, and the counter had been forced to decline. Now, though - maybe he could use his findings to make a case for the cost. He could have spotted the mothers earlier.

***

Three nights later, he thought he had found the reason. He had gone through the birth records, talked to the mothers, taking notes. Where had they given birth? What had they eaten? Who was attending? What was the father doing?

It was the natal attendant.

_A_ natal attendant.

A young man, working at the foundry.

No children of his own, even, but the mothers seemed to like him. He had a calming presence, they’d said.

Well, his presence wasn’t what made babies live, the counter thought, sipping at his ale.

He didn’t know what did. But he was the only thing the mothers had in common, as far as he could see.

***

He met the young man at the foundry the next morning. A boy, more like.

He was unloading charcoal from a cart when the counter arrived, and introduced himself. The boy flushed his hands in a bucket of water and then wiped them on his apron.

‘My apologies,’ he said, extending his hand. ‘Don’t want to get coal and soot on you.’

The counter shook the boy’s hand and then looked at his. It had black stains nonetheless.

‘Sorry,’ the boy said again.

‘I’m saving what soap we have for engraving work. Can’t have soot or oil there.’

He had kept on talking while they walked into the foundry. The counter found himself liking the boy, but something else was bothering him.

The boy gestured again, and the counter saw it—black in the creases, stubborn, clinging. Not surface dirt. Something that stayed.

Fevers did not come from air. 

They came from contact.

***

The numbers were off, but he had expected as much. He had brought up his first observations in the hall. The fire had burned in the pit in the center, casting the faces around in an orange and red relief. He had talked through the steps he had taken, and showed them the numbers. They had taken notes, asked a few questions.

In the end, he had recommended that all birth-attendants wash their hands with soap before and after each attendance. There seemed to be a sensitivity to fouling from other processes. He did not know why, but he trusted the numbers. Perhaps it was innate to babies, or perhaps it was the process of birthing itself. The numbers didn’t lie, and the numbers showed that.

There had been opposition, of course. The old smith suggested, clearing his throat, they do nothing but watch the situation for a few more years. Not because he wanted to, the counter thought, but because somebody was expected to be the voice of caution. They might waste expensive soap otherwise.

Others had spoken of a careful approach. Have soap given to a few more attendants in midtown and lowtown. Record the numbers, compare them. Then speak again. The voices for taking a step at a time, for planning. They were never short of speakers.

There had been eager voices, too. The cartographer had wanted soap for doctors and bakers, too. Everybody who touched living flesh or foodstuff. The counter chuckled at the thought. The girl was never afraid to open her mouth.

In the end, they had aligned very close to his original idea. Babies were tomorrow’s smiths and farm workers, and the cost of delaying could be bad. Soap would be bought from the next trader to arrive, and the coin chest would bear the cost. If it didn’t work, taxes would slowly fill it back over the next few years. If it worked, it would actually take longer, but then pay off massively as the kids reached working age.

Now, the beads told a clear story. Before, the lowest groove - pregnancies - had had the widest string of beads of them all. Now, the next one was nearly as wide.

The counter smiled, savoring his ale.

Doctors, he mused.

And bakers. And anyone who touched food, or wounds, or tools that passed between hands.

The beads would widen again.

Or they would learn why not.

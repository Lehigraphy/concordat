They would burn the bodies with the others. Perhaps the city would live.

**

The plague had come with the meltwaters. An affliction of the gut. Fever followed, within a week.

Then, death.

The water was causing it. It had turned bad and foul-smelling, and the sickness had followed soon. They had found the underground cesspool, overflown and leaking into the soil. 

They had realized that the brewery people were spared. They filtered and boiled their water, to improve ale they said.

All had been too late.

Half the city was sick already.

**

The wallbreakers were washing their swords and armor, helms still on.

**

The trader had arrived a few days after the first cases. Said he had mold that cured the plague, and parchments telling how to make more. 

The sample had seemed to work. 

The price had been too high. More than they had coin. More than they could barter away and live.

They had offered to pay later. More, even, in the next three springs.

The trader had declined. Said the city might still die, that it might be too late already—and he would be ruined with it.

**

They were standing in line again, helmed and spotless.

**

That night, he had called the wallbreakers. Quietly.

They had stood in line. Helmed, indistinguishable. Some hadn’t yet removed their rings or talismans. He’d tried not to look.

The plates had reflected his face, stretched and blurred.

He had picked one in three.

Enough to be certain.

The others, he had sent home.

The third, to the sleeping caravan.

None had hesitated. All had friends and family sick.

**

The wallbreakers had gone now. One by one. Unknown.

The bottles of mold were in front of him, reflecting. The parchments, too.

The city might still live.

The cost would not.

The maker held the beginning and the end on his cupped palms. Both weighed the same, after all that was the point of their very existence. One thousand grains, or a _kilo_ as they were supposed to be called today. The same as a tall pint of ale, or two dozen eggs. He knew the beginning was accurate to one tenth of a grain. By the feel of it, the end was too, he had to admit.

Both were polished cylinders of steel, the size of a stone pine cone, with a handle on top. He could feel the hammer marks on the left one. It was one of the very first made by his second-oldest son, his taps still heavy on the left. There were a few corrections from his eldest, barely perceptible. His daughter’s engraving covered the entire surface. The old sawmill as it was seen from downstream, the big waterwheel centered on the side. The river bend curved around the bottom, the waterlilies wrapping all the way to the other side. He had carved his mark himself near the neck, name and day and number of weight made.

The second weight had been cast, lathed at the new mill, and then hardened. There was the forge symbol on one side, and the one-capital-K stamping on the other.

The man had brought it.

He sat opposite the maker, all fine-stitched low-valley cloth and hands that had never held a hammer. A _counter_. The clasp holding back his oiled, shoulder-length hair was silver, and of decent make, he had to admit.

He had offered the maker a payment. A good payment. A good chunk of ploughable land, and a small but untapped vein of copper higher up. Together, it was probably worth more than he had made in the last two decades, and it would continue providing for his children and children’s children for a generation to come.

He was going to tear the parchment to shreds. He would go fetch his hammer and drive the man out of his shop and halfway to the river. He would piss at the man’s feet and then curse his entire bloodline to the bogs.

The maker sighed, slumping. His anger went as quickly as it had come. The man was a bearer of news, nothing more. He had invited the counter in himself, and there were rules of hospitality to follow.

***

His temper was a trait shared by most of his people up-valley. It was why he had made his first weight, nearly three dozen years ago when he was still a jeweller and not a weight maker. It had been the month of harvest, but a chill wind from the mountains had destroyed a quarter of the barley crop earlier that summer. He had happened on the miller, nearly at blows with the requisitioner from a low-valley storehouse.

‘That wretch has tampered with the weights, made them lighter,’ he had turned to the maker, spittle flying. The maker had taken half a step back, on instinct.

‘I don’t–’ the other man had begun, but the miller had bounced back at him, shoulders hunched like a mountain bear.

‘You do and you have, you wretch! By the gods will I–.’ 

It had taken the maker the best part of an hour and several pints of beer to extract what had happened. That spring, the miller had bought tools and salt from the storehouse, with a signed promise to pay in barley come harvest. Now the requisitioner had come to collect, but had measured more grain than the miller had reckoned.

‘How much?’ The maker had cut in as the requisitioner drew breath, before the talks got heated again.

‘Not much,’ the miller had grumpled. ‘One barrel in twenty. But it is bad. So much already lost.’ He had stared at his pint.

The requisitioner had offered to only take half of what was owed now, with the remainder and a fifth more the next year. The crops had been fine down-valley and mid, he had told. A fair offer.

‘What’s the point?’ the miller had complained, slumping over the table. ‘He just changes the weights. Again. Takes all we have.’

‘What if I make you a weight?’ the maker had offered.
‘Why? He’ll change it all the same.’ The requisitioner had pinched the bridge of his nose at that, shaking his head. ‘I engrave it, everywhere. He can’t cut away anything without you noticing. Nobody carves like I do, not around here. Ask your wife.’

The miller had raised his eyes from his pint at that point, thoughtful. The requisitioner had cleared his throat, though.
‘You could make another, bigger one, while I’m gone. Say that’s the real one,’ he had ventured, raising his eyebrows. The miller’s face had begun to turn red, and the man had quickly clarified.
‘Not saying you will, I say. Only that you could. It’s still a weight.’

The miller’s eyes had lit up.
‘Make two,’ he had exclaimed, knocking over an empty pint. ‘One for me, one for…’ He waved his hand in the other man’s direction. ‘We sign the numbers using them. Then compare them, next harvest, to see that they match.’

The miller had waved for another beer, and the requisitioner had looked thoughtful.

‘But anything you buy from me’, he pointed, ‘we use the same weights.’ Then he added, after a moment.
‘And I want to use the same pairs with the smithies and farms here, as well. You’ll have to explain to them how it works.’

‘Aye. That I can do.’ The miller had taken a gulp from his new pint. ‘But nowhere in the Hells am I paying you a fifth more.’ The maker sighed.

***

‘You should be proud.’

The man with the silver clasp continued talking. The piece of parchment lay on the table, untouched.

‘All three valleys have aligned. Merchants and traders will use the new weights within a year. Forgery will be punishable, mind.’ He nodded at the maker. ‘Deals will be struck faster. Who knows this better than you?’

The maker thought about fetching his hammer, rules of hospitality be damned. He put the engraved weight down on his desk instead, his fist clenching tight immediately afterward.

The other man craned his neck to see through the window, as if to get his bearings.
‘And it’s not just the three valleys,’ he continued, his hand sweeping. ‘The biggest mines further upstream are already using your pairs. Well, at least when trading with us. With others too, I would wager. And so are the coal-makers behind the Two Passes.‘ 

The maker was aware of that. He had spoken to a miner once, when an ore barge had sailed down. The man had come looking for a gift for his daughter’s first apprenticeship, and had recognized a weight. Mines often needed tools and timber before a single pick had hit a new vein, the maker had learned. A shared pair made agreeing on a promise-trade easier, for the amount of ore to be barged later as payment.

The counter took a moment to adjust his clasp, face practically beaming with excitement. Damn but the man could talk a lot. Were all down-valley people like this, or just the counters?

‘We reckon trade will grow by a tenth. A fifth, even. All thanks to your idea.’ A smile, eyebrows raised. ‘Prices will be better. We’ll have enough ore to light the big foundry again.’

He gave the maker an expectant look. The maker glowered back, but his anger was waning already. Weariness was creeping in, like frost on the windows after harvest. He turned the machined weight in his hands, idly, without realizing.

‘You have earned that,’ the man added, nodding at the parchment. ‘And it’s not like we are putting you out of business. If anything, your weights will probably sell for more than they did before. Everybody will use the same measures, but yours are masterful craft compared to that.’ 

He nodded at the lathe-cut lump of steel in the maker’s hands. 

‘You can keep that in a hall over the hearth. I read that a survey-caravan of ours had seen one in the keep of a valley king.’ He smiled at the title. ‘Kept it there for any local trade disputes. We have no idea how it ended that far. We have never traded with them.’

The maker knew exactly who had.

***

The trader had sailed from upstream, two decades ago. The maker had just been about to close for the day when the man had walked in, his blue-dyed beard braided full of rings and a war-axe on his back. He had come for his jewellery, turning a golden bracelet in his fingers when he noticed the short row of weights behind the desk.

‘You make?’ he had asked, dipping his head to gesture at the weights, and raising an eyebrow. The wrinkles on his forehead deepened as he did. ‘For…buy and sell machine?’ The rings clinked as his chin moved.

‘For a scale, yes.’ He had explained the idea to the trader. Then he explained it again, taking care with the words. They didn’t have that many in common, so he had moved the weights around on his desk in demonstration, gesturing with his hands and playing out the roles of a buyer and a trader. In the end, the bearded man’s face had cleared in understanding. 

‘This is good. The word of ice-born not always enough’, he had said, slamming his fist on the desk for emphasis. Then he nodded to himself and continued  ‘Leave weight in town, to king or chief-woman, return next snow. Weight same.’ 

Yes, they were using them across the valley. He had sold over ten pairs by then. Even the biggest low-valley storehouse had one. The carvings had turned from the original curls and arcs into valleys and people. His oldest was already practicing the craft.

‘How much coin?,’ the trader had asked, pointing at the row of weights.

‘Thirty silver for a pair.’ The maker had lifted two weights to the table, and demonstrated the sum with his fingers and a coin. He could have asked for more, he wagered, but making the trade was more important than the coin. The boat-traders travelled far and saw a lot, and having one carry his weights would be the talk of the valley for some time.

‘No. Too much.’ The maker had smiled back politely, and prepared for a long session of bargaining. As expected. He had seen people of different traditions pass through the valley, but none liked to haggle like the ice-born. But the trader had continued before he’d got his mouth open.

‘Two-ten of silver for two weights,’ he said, his palm thumping against the desk. ‘A pair of weights, yes? I will buy two-ten pairs. Pay you four hundred silver.’ A show of fingers.

The maker blinked, leaning forwards. He must have misunderstood. It usually took him months to make that much coin. A year, if the crops were bad or a vein ran empty.

‘I only have six pairs. It takes three weeks to make a pair. A moon, almost,’ he added, gesturing at the weight and his workbench.

‘Then I take six pairs. Come back later, and then I take four and ten. You make when I’m away.’

‘You’ll have to pay me in advance, though. Pay me now’, he’d clarified after the trader’s questioning expression. ‘Sometimes traders never come back.’ It would take him years to sell fourteen pairs if that happened.

‘But if you disappear? Take my silver and go?’

‘So the word of a valley-born is not enough now?’ the maker had asked, smiling.

He had woken up slumped over his table the next morning, with the worst bottle-ache in ages. The weights had gone, and a pile of silver had replaced them on the table. Silver, and empty bottles of brown glass. Mied, the trader had called it. Or moid, or something. Just thinking about the liquid made his stomach turn. He wouldn’t be making jewellery today.

***

Now, the table was empty apart from the parchment. There were no empty bottles, but the maker had a sour taste in his mouth all the same. His signature had already dried on the parchment.

The counter had left hours ago. He sat alone, only the faint clinking from the workshop breaking the silence. His children were almost done with work today, and would be joining him shortly.

He tried telling himself it was the sensible choice. His daughter would never have forgiven him, had he rejected. ‘You prideful old fool’, she would have called him. Not angry, or yelling, just…sad, making it a thousand times worse. She had always been good with the numbers, and the numbers did not bend .

The maker cupped the weights in his palms again. The lathed one was well made, he conceded. Perfectly symmetric, and he could tell the surface would crack if you tried to cut or drill it. Difficult to alter.

His shoulders fell with his exhale. It had been inevitable. Good ideas always spread, and were taken to use. His name had already been carved in the wall of inventions. Whoever first turned iron into steel wouldn’t have felt this way.

And the low-valley man had been right. His sons and daughter could keep making engraved weights, and sell them for more than ever before. He was sure of that.

But they wouldn’t be _his_ any more.

The High King watched the besieging force with irritation. The foundry people had outsmarted him, and he prided himself on being smart. It was how he had ascended the throne before his brother, after all.

They had used the storm to cover their approach, and probably hunted his sentries down with light horsemen. That must be it.

The foundries had declared war a fortnight ago. The king couldn't fault them for being dishonorable, just...sly. He had expected a series of campaigns over his three valleys, but instead they had come straight here. For him.

He had expected them at the valley passes, or the river crossings. Not here. Not first.

Still, they couldn't hope to win. He had already sent pigeons to his bannermen, and relief would be arriving in a few days. In the meantime, they only had to beat them back at the walls. It would be bloody, but war always was.

**

The ram kept creeping closer. Arrows and rocks simply bounced from the angled plates. Once, a ballista had hit a plate straight-on and penetrated half-way, but the thing hadn't even faltered in its approach.

At the same time, the foundries had clearly brought enough archers to make any sallying force pay dearly, even if they managed to stop the machine. They would reach the gatehouse and breach the gate. The king's men would simply have to beat them back from the gateyard.

They could do that. The warriors inside the ram would be exhausted after hauling what must surely be tonnes of iron uphill.

**

The drawbar splintered, and the gate was pried open. The High King's men took up his battlecry.

**

The High King swung his poleaxe, bringing down his opponent. _By the gods_ they took some killing. The blow hadn't even pierced the helm, he thought, panting. The man-at-arms would be getting up come night, with nothing but a nasty headache.

They were _losing_.

The men that had burst from the splintered gate were fully covered in plate, yet moved as though it weren't even there.

They weren't even that skilled. Oh they were good, but nothing the king hadn't seen before. Nothing he hadn't beaten.

But they wouldn't go down.

Swords and axes slid off the plate, and mauls bounced back.

A lucky strike might find a gap or bring a concussion, but he was losing five men for every one of theirs. Even the Hallguard was going down, men he had known and trained with since his birth.

The High King took a step back, the Hallguard immediately stepping in to fill the gap.

They had already lost.

The High King could see that now.

They would all die here.

His bannermen would arrive late. They would annihilate the foundry army, yes—and bleed for it.

And then there would be no one left to hold the valleys together. His kingdom would become leaderless and with too few defenders. In all likelihood, the banners would turn on each other.

He could see that now.

The eyes of the High King searched the enemy troops.

There.

A warrior, no, a soldier. Nothing on him, really. But he had the movements, pointing, the line following with him. On a closer look, three small banners on his gorget.

The king yelled a command, and the Hallguard disengaged, mauls and poleaxes on guard.

Another shout, pointing with his polearm. The enemy commander turned his head.

The king lowered his polearm, then raised his visor.

The enemy soldier raised a hand.

The line stopped.

Slowly, using his polearm to support his exhausted legs, the High King sank to his right knee. Slowly at first, then more and more, his men did the same.

The man with the three hammers nodded once.

**

More of his men would live than he had feared. Those with pierced guts or bubbling chests were granted mercy when they wished, but those with cuts and tears were...getting better. The foundry army had brought healers with them, and insisted on treating his warriors in addition to theirs. They had washed the wounds, and then covered them with bandages stinking of cellar and old bread. He had been aghast at such practices, but he wasn't really in a position to decline. Impossibly, very few of the wounds had festered.

The High King sat on his throne, signing his seal and authority on a parchment. Agreed quotas of copper and iron. Beneath the steps leading up to his throne, a bearded man in plain robes waited. Not a warrior or a soldier, from his bearing and frame. The man did not look up as he spoke.

He remained king. That was the term.

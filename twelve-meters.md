An ornate timepiece on Zora's desk showed it was past ten already. It was dark outside her window, and the offices were quiet. She had imported tea in her cup, but the mounting ache behind her eyes was entirely homegrown.

She was skimming through the report one final time. The battle of the Weiter Pass had begun like any alignment operation. A preparatory shelling of the Conglomerate positions, and then a simultaneous strike on six separate sites. Three full companies, nine breacher squads.

They had cracked open the enemy strongpoints, destroyed the ground forces, and then fallen back. Anvil had moved in to consolidate, began bulldozing their star of trenches and deploying prefabricated pillboxes. A brief mortar duel had ensued, indecisive but in Concordat's favour, the superior range of their weapons forcing the enemy to relocate. The Conglomerate had then smoked the entire eastern valley, forcing the Concordat drones higher and cutting visual contact to the ground. Ground command had assumed they were covering their retreat.

Then, the trap had been sprung. The Conglomerate counterattack had hit Strongpoint D at the worst possible moment, with Hammer almost back to base but Anvil not yet fully dug in. A real rock-slide of men and tanks, rolling in from the banks of smoke.

The enemy had learned guile. Strongpoint D had been a ruse, empty fuel tanks and second-grade equipment, defended by their slave soldiers. _Tithed men_, she corrected herself, her lip curling up a fraction.

The smoke had covered their approach. A good tactic, Zora had to add. _Their_ tactic.

Within five minutes, the Anvil positions had been close to being overrun.

Zora sipped at her tea and pulled up the video of a captured enemy officer. The man was sitting in the interrogation chamber, ramrod-straight and jaw set, sidearm still on belt. His cheek and left hand were still bandaged. Burn scars sustained when he was trapped in a disabled personnel carrier, according to supplement 2.18.

[Captain Kaherr, 3rd mechanized regiment, 1st company], told the video overlay. The man was looking straight at the interrogator.

'There were three sacrificial sites, according to the Lords.' ['Truth, 85 %], said the overlay. The man continued.

'You could have attacked _any_ of them, and we'd still have mashed you.' the captain told.

[Lie, 87 %], told the overlay. Zora agreed. They had sieved through all their pre-attack intelligence, yes, and identified those two extra sites that Kaherr spoke about. Two of them had been suboptimally situated, and any counterattack would have arrived either piecemeal, or with Anvil properly dug in.

Strongpoint D had been the perfect trap for the enemy. And they had fallen for it, thought Zora. They should have seen the pieces in place.

On the terminal, the captain went on, now gesturing vividly. He was claiming the Concordat walked right into their trap, that the Conglomerate almost beat them this time, absolute certainty in his voice.

[Lie], told the overlay. Zora wasn't so certain.

No, Anvil had been close to breaking. And she wouldn't blame them for that. She'd seen the debriefs of the few shell-shocked survivors, and the men and women had been through a grinder. Half a minute more, and they might have made a run for it. A few minutes more, and there wouldn't have been anybody left to run.

Then the Hammer had struck.

Zora opened another video, this one drone footage. An assault carrier was partially obscured by smoke, its plough down and sparking with impacts. It had been the first one to arrive. A green pointer on the screen indicated the bearing of the second one, a timer next to it counting down from forty-five.

Another pointer, this one glowing red. The estimated bearing and time of arrival of the Conglomerate second wave. Twenty seconds.

Zora held her breath, despite knowing the outcome.

Impossibly, the Conglomerate attack had not faltered at first, the soldiers hunkering in craters and behind burning vehicles, yet returning fire. Zora suspected they had been near-insensate from the violence at this point.

The video panned left, towards the southern flank. The enemy line there was almost entirely covered in smoke, but not completely.

The video zoomed in. Troopers, hunkering in a crater, working their bolt-action rifles.

Next, something spun through the air, green and rectangular, tumbling end over end, with a dull metallic clatter as it hit the ground. It landed right next to an enemy combat engineer, evident from their dark blue armband. The man startled back, dropping his gun.

Then, he turned, and ran.

Then, another. Then, a squad. A platoon.

Zora watched the enemy line break, like a bridge collapsing beam by beam. 

Within seconds, the entire line was routing. Personnel carriers reversed and turned, colliding with and blocking the tanks of the second wave. The green timer counted to zero.

Zora released her breath.

Without the rout, the second wave would have overwhelmed the single breacher squad and the Anvil positions. In all likelihood, the second squad would have been forced to fall back. It was well possible they could have flooded enough troops into the breach to roll the whole Concordat line, costing them the other five Anvil companies and the entire valley.

The military and economical cost would have been bad.

The blow on morale would have been devastating.

She skipped to another video, a short middle-aged man with broad shoulders. Captive 10934 [Trooper Bauer, self-identified / unverified], laughing at something, in silence until the audio popped back. His patched uniform was getting tight around the midsection. He'd been caught near the northern flank, the last part of the enemy line to collapse.

'Ay, we saw them terror tank appear alright. Rolled up from the smog like a nightmare. Nearly shit meself when they did.'

The man was quiet for a moment, before continuing.

'Reckoned they was bursting with iron walkers inside surely. I saw someone ran, and I ran after 'em. You can bet I did, the same second, boss. Ran like the rock devils 'emselves was behind me, ready to carry me poor soul to hell. Aint no shame in that, boss. Kept me gun tho, the bastards would shoot me ass if I lost me gun.'

Again, the man went silent. This, his face lit up and he continued.

'Would have gotten away, too. I was almost back at the smoke, but then I got me leg stuck in zoggen spikewire. Didn't notice the bastard thing, I was too busy looking over me shoulder. Bit me leg like a 'hornbush, it did.'
 
Despite herself, Zora smiled. On her terminal, trooper Bauer went on.
 
'Wonder you could find me a smoke, boss?'

She closed the video, massaging her eyes with her palms. She had been putting off the hardest part, but now she had no excuses left. She'd have to finalize her recommendations.

She went through the easiest one first — no last-minute revisions there. A recommendation to begin design and development of a mobile rapid response and reconnaissance platform. She had small four-wheelers in mind, but operational requirements would decide the final shape. They could be their eyes in smoke and woodlands, and punch out weakly defended positions like Strongpoint D had been.

Hammer would no longer have exclusive access to suits. Not unless they could craft a personnel carrier with enough terrain capability, and she suspected the physics would not allow. She smiled. The Commander-Master of Breaching would have a fit. But, he would see the numbers in the end, as he always did. She had to hand it to the oaf.

She set a reminder for herself to go visit the workshop, if her proposal was accepted. She had spent far too much time on the proposal, mentally outlining the system she had in mind. Development was supposed to be Hammer business today, the best utilization of native talent. She'd be there to _provide Furnace-doctrical oversight_. Nobody would say no. Being an old flare of effluent had its perks.

Now, on to the harder topic. The Conglomerate had convincingly demonstrated the value of minimally trained troops as expendable diversions and tarpits. They could utilize the same tactic in the recently-aligned regions. 

She didn't pretend to understand the social psychology of the Conglomerate states and whatever made their subjects consent to their tithes. Whatever it was, it probably wouldn't work for the Concordat. Still initial social surveys suggested that offering a rather minimal pay would lead sufficient recruitment numbers. Hells, even offering three warm meals a day would work in the poorer states.

The Journeyman she'd tasked to analysing this approach had held the report like something foul when he'd presented it. A soft core, that one had. She wasn't yet sure if the positives outweighed the negatives or vice versa. But, the numbers didn't lie, and he'd stayed true to them as far as she could tell, she nodded.

The idea _had_ merit. Even a few hundred extra troopers acting as a damper in Weiter would have--

No. She wouldn't do it. Behavioural projections had large margins of error, and they couldn't afford an insurgency now. Besides, any conscript would have a been an apprentice in a year and a half, often even faster.

The Journeyman would sleep better now, she estimated.

No, she'd go with the second option. Increase the coverage of preparatory bombardment by thirty percent, and suppression by twenty-five. To support the entire logistical chain for the nitrogen flux needed, they'd need to open the secondary stockpiles of steel...

She stopped herself. The summary section alone was several pages long, and going through the tables and models at this hour would achieve precisely nothing. The logistics option it was.

Unfortunately, it would also lock in the third option on her report.

Morale in the Central-Eastern axis was already strained. She'd spoken to the First Master of Yields, and the numbers on steel were concerning. With the strain from supporting the nitrogen industry, they'd get worse. The consumer goods shortages would worsen, and the prices would keep on rising. They only had to hold until they got the refineries in Karsik and Koharsk running, but that was at least ten months away. At least copper was now recovering, she thought.

Still, the news from Weiter could trigger an inflation event, a hoarding panic, or any of the five other unpleasant scenarios listed in Appendix 8.3.

If there _were_ news from Weither. She grimaced, from a stab of pain between her ears now.

She had been raised to value the exchange of knowledge. Apprentices learned from the masters and journeymen, and later became masters themselves, adding their learnings to the craft.

 Granted, there were guilds and patents and embargo periods...Maybe they could frame it as the latter. Send the telegrams and release the numbers as normal, but classify the detailed reports for the entire arc of alignment. Just for a year, citing operational security. The numbers would look like a large-scale concerted push, and witholding the reports from the entire arc would suggest the very same.
 
 The numbers would be all correct. The conclusions of the public would be all wrong.
 
 Her tea had gone cold and bitter.
 
 When they released the full reports, they would have lost much of their shock value. The steel situation would be improving by then. Everyday goods would be back on shelves. _Her baker could get his oven refurbished._ She'd have to drop him a hint, encourage him to wait a little.
 
 She was about to sign when she realized she'd forgotten something. Sloppy, she shouldn't be working so late anymore.
 
 She pulled up the captain section again, interview report verification subsection. Drone ventral camera footage, zoomed and grainy, the perspective slowly shifting as the flier made its pass. A breacher was leaning against a smoking wreck of an APC, its green camouflage burned black at spots. The breacher was straining to get a warped rear hatch open. The hatch tore free and spun away, tumbling end over end. The microphone array picked up a dull clatter as it hit something inside the smoke.
 
 The breacher reached in, pulling out the captain. A girl of seventeen on her second deployment, she read, her eyes straying to the photograph on her desk.
 
 She'd been out of alignment by almost twelve meters. The girl had ignored commands to fall back, citing communications failure in her debrief. Enemy EW had still been active, and the radio analysts had rated squad-level links reliability  at fifty-seven percent at the time. _Confidence interval forty to sixty-six._
 
 The analyst adjusted the numbers down by ten percentage points, referring to operational...no, probability of obscurant-induced RF scattering in her comment.
 
 She leaned back against her chair, closing her eyes.
 
 The timepiece on her table gonged eleven. It no longer kept the time, so it was past midnight already.
 
 Her signature dried quietly at the bottom of the report.

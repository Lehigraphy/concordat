The dam seemed to stretch all the way to the horizon. To Rhel, it was as good as home. He had been a powerplant technician all his life, like his father and grandfather had been. The infrasonic thrum of the thousands of tonnes of water passing through the turbines was the heartbeat of his life.

Rhel had never seen so many men and vehicles at once. The men of Karsik Gorge were used to tithes: power every day, grain at Harvest. A month ago, the tithe had been men. They told him that the nation of blacksmiths and iron-makers was coming. Well, they could try. The men of Karsik would kneel for nobody. There were tithes, yes, but they were agreed upon. You either agreed, or you rebelled and then you won or then you died. That was honorable. Kneeling was not.

A gun felt foreign in his hands, but he had seen enough war films to know that the Gorge was impassable. The walls were too rocky and steep, and the dam was over eight hundred meters long, completely exposed. He could not begin to imagine what all those men were meant to stop.

Then the storm came. It began with the fog, rolling down from the mountains and drifting over the reservoir. Rhel had never seen fog like that before. It stung the eyes and burned in the sinuses. Perhaps it was the obscurant the instructors had mentioned.

Then came the explosions. They had been waiting in the trenches for what felt like hours, ever since the fog appeared, but Rhel still didn’t feel prepared at all. The noise defied understanding, stealing the air from his lungs and turning the world into constant ringing. Pebbles fell onto his vest. Pieces of concrete, he noted absently. From the main yard, judging by the grain and the color of the sand. He felt the displaced air of something passing overhead. The tower exploded.

Then came the thunder. Rhel did not hear it—he could hear nothing. He felt it in his feet and belly and arms, the deepest rumble carried through the dam and into the trench. Just like a chipped turbine blade.

The violence stopped, and Rhel risked a glance over the trench. Through the thinning fog, he could make out the outline of a vehicle unlike any he had ever seen, roaring over the dam. It was close, no more than two or three hundred meters away. The thing looked like a malformed farm tractor, with a plough three times bigger than anybody could ever need and no cabin for a driver. Then another appeared from the fog. He knew he was going to die. They were all going to die.

A second passed. Another. No more vehicles appeared. Maybe the third one had hit a mine farther down the dam and was now blocking the others? Gunfire lashed out to meet the two. Rhel told himself they could live after all. The vehicles could not possibly carry more than twenty men each. They still had hundreds. The big rockets carried by the army men would destroy the ridiculous tractors.

As if on cue, one flashed out and exploded against the plough of the lead vehicle. Then another. Still, the thing kept coming. Maybe it was dead inside and just rolling on sheer inertia?

The vehicle slowed and came to a stop almost on top of the first trench, the one filled with tithed men. The plough crashed down, the impact carried through the concrete. Smoke began to rise again, shrouding the machine.

Then, impossibly, something came out. It looked like a man made of iron but left unfinished, like the clay figures his cousin started and never completed. Its proportions were obscene, bulked beyond the limits of flesh and muscle. The thing carried a door-sized slab of steel as if it were a sheet of roofing or a tray from the kitchens.

It was almost at the first trench now. Rhel thought he could see gunfire striking it, little puffs and sparks that did not even slow it.

Then, against all sense, it stepped over the trench, as if the men in it were beneath notice. Rhel saw another monster follow in its wake. This one stopped at the trench, turning what looked like a colossal gun along its length. Then the smoke swallowed them both. Yellow flashes bloomed inside it. Then, nothing.

A shaky breath. Another. Then the nightmare began for real.

The monster stepped out of the smoke. Up close—so terrifyingly close—Rhel could see that it was also carrying a gun in its free hand, something like the weapons he had seen mounted on tripods, behind sandbags and on off-road vehicles. Seeing it carried in a single hand made no sense.

The iron monster began working its gun across the width of the trench, shot by shot. Each retort sent a meter-long tongue of flame from the barrel. Rhel felt every shot as a wash of hot air across his face. Soon it would turn on him and it would be over. Worst of all, the monster never stopped walking. He wanted to run more than he had ever wanted anything in his life, but the men of Karsik did not run. Another shot, and something wet touched his face.

Then, a miracle. Two, no three, pan-sized flyers in the sky. _Drones_, from the army, with four propellers and an explosive charge. They dived for the monsters, like a falcon catching a mouse. Something flashed on the monster’s back and the first flier spiraled away. Again, and the second machine shattered, much closer than the first.

The third one struck the monster and exploded. That close, the blast was strong enough to throw Rhel off balance, and he tumbled to the bottom of the trench.

When he clambered back up, the monster lay face-down on the concrete.

Rhel had never seen anything so beautiful in his entire life.

Just as quickly, the moment vanished. The monster stirred. Picking. Itself. Up.

It could not be happening. It could not. It was dead. It had to be dead.

First an elbow. Then a knee. It lifted its gun. Its slab of steel—only now did Rhel understand that it was a shield. A shield, like the ones carried by mounted knights in the stories from hundreds of years ago.

The monster stood again and seemed to look straight at Rhel, and only at Rhel, as if the rest of the Gorge did not exist. As if the world did not exist.

Then Rhel did what no man of Karsik had done for two hundred years. Slowly, ever so slowly, he sank to his knees at the bottom of the trench. He knelt. Hands on his legs, head bowed, as men knelt when honor failed. His gun lay on the ground. He did not remember dropping it.

He felt the earth shift and saw the shadow of the monster pass over him. It stopped. It growled and hissed, like a crane lifting a new turbine wheel. Something flickered across him, as if it were judging him, weighing his worth, deciding whether to condemn or to consume.

Rhel found the last of his defiance and lifted his eyes a fraction. The feet of the monster stood on the trench lip. They were steel. Cracked and scratched and pitted, but steel. Good steel. Rhel knew steel.

The monster moved on.

Rhel remained kneeling.

He was still kneeling when it grew quiet.

He was still kneeling when the quiet broke again, this time with the sound of trucks.

The next day, the power plant was again producing electricity, new cables and transformers ferried in and quickly installed. The walls of the control building were now concrete. The fields were tended. The tithes remained, but now they were required.

The next week, the thunder began again, in the valley beyond the Gorge.

The monster was hungry.

## Forebear
The forebear stepped on his longboat, the carved rings in his beard clinking. Each metal and engraving told a story to anyone who could read it. Of deals struck, of debts owed and claimed, of children born and lost.

A bell was tolling, wishing him farewell and a safe journey, he hoped. The water was reflecting the town palisade, and a few children were waving from the tower.  The boat was lightly loaded. They would make good speed, even on oars and against the stream. 

He had sat with the people of the smithy around their communal fire, sharing bread and salt, bargaining and negotiating. They had offered him swords, axe heads, and armor plates unlike anything he had ever seen. First, he had laughed at their flimsy sheets, saying that they’d crack in anything like real use. Then, with permission, he had taken his best war axe on a plate, an overhead death-blow.

The axe bounced right back.

The forebear had only carried half a boatload of salted fish at that point. Not nearly enough to trade for what he wanted.

It had taken lots of talking, but he had convinced the smithy people in the end. His people, the ice-born, had nothing but their longboats, their children, and their reputation. Break one, and the whole family dies. His word was his bond.

He had paid for his current cargo with nothing but a promise. Three swords for himself and his people, fifteen axeheads and two crates of plates to trade. It was a fraction of his usual cargo, but it would pay more than two or three voyages.

While returning downstream, he would lend his boat and his crew to the smithy people, ferrying down two boatloads of pig iron from two valleys upstream. From the river bend.

He would honor his promise.

He had to.

The river always remembered, and so did the forebear.

## Boy

The valley whispered, his father said. They were sitting on the river bank near the bend, his father chewing on some leaves, their trucks parked some distance away. The river babbled, and the breeze was rustling the reeds.  They had arrived two days ago, and would be going again early next morning.

The valley spoke to everybody who listened, his father continued. Of the winter, of the spring to come. Just like the father might speak to the valley people, he’d said, of the foundry people about to arrive in a spring or two. _For the right price._

They had parked on the town square, and the townspeople had arrived, as they always did. They had seemed barely taller than the boy, which was weird. His father almost seemed as tall as the trucks they rode in.

They had been carrying ploughs and axeheads, and some grain. The townspeople had traded in jewellery, embroidered cloths, and dirty bottles of yellow-tinted water that made his uncle cough and grimace. The trinkets had looked nice to the boy, but he’d later seen his father toss them to the scrap box in the back of the truck.

The boy asked if the trade was good here.

‘No,’ his father said.

‘Barely worth the stop.’

‘Then why do we come?’

‘Because the tide ebbs and flows for everyone,’ the father replied, mysteriously.

‘And the people remember,’ he added, smiling. ‘No other crew is stopping here. When the ice melts for these people, we’ll pull a much better price.’

‘Now go help your mother cook in the hús-trailer.’

## Man

The valley spoke in quiet, nervous tones. There were craters on the fields around the town, and something huge and heavy had torn off cobblestones from the road. The air smelled of upturned earth and smoke. When they arrived, a man with a black helmet had forbidden them from the town square, but then added that they could park near the river bend.

The man was trading with the townspeople. They had come, carefully in ones and twos first, but then as a stream, like they always did. The people barely reached his neck now, but many of them had clean clothes. They bought spices and cheap jewellery from a few valleys upstream, paying with foundry coin and moonshine. He’d have to hide the booze from his uncle.

He could wear his first ring after this town, on his finger until his beard grew thick enough. There would be laughter, and then mead. He had already met kings and tyrants, with his father.

He had not yet met one here.

The river bend gurgled behind a gun nest.

Truck tracks cut the mud.

## Father

The valley screamed, the father was telling his son. Rings rattled on his beard as his chin moved.

‘When will the tide ebb here?’ the boy asked. He’d told him about the tide a few moments ago.

Go fetch my toolbox,’ the father said instead. ‘We’ll mount the new figurehead on the cab-bow.’ 

The river had been reduced to a trickle, the muddy bed exposed and wet. The town administrator had welcomed them in, and then warned his son not to venture too far from the bend. They would be increasing the flow shortly, and the river might return quickly.

The mountain face behind the opposite bank was dominated by the mines. Conveyors carried huge boulders to machines that turned them into pebbles. Even here, it was a low, constant thunder.

The rumble of trucks drowned whatever other sounds were left. He had spoken to the drivers in the town, paying for  a few drinks and coffees. They were carrying ore from the mines to the smelteries, he’d learned, and then iron and steel to the valleys downstream.

He had seen other conquered valleys before, and traded with the oppressors and oppressed alike. Usually, they took what they wanted and left. These ones stayed.

And built.

Trade had been good. After they had arrived, they had spent a full day on each of the three primary markets of the city. The people had sold handcrafted jewellery and decorated timepieces, and bought wine from the southern harbours and tea from the east. The elderly were short, as he remembered from his youth. The young could have passed for any iceborn, though.

## Elder

The valley was silent. The elder was sitting on a bench, watching. The river was straight now, the banks paved with piers and brick. The valley had stopped speaking a long time ago, the man thought. But maybe the people had learned to live without listening.

They had loaded their trucks on a cargo train early in the afternoon. His crew spent all morning helping to unload the crates of components from the train, to pay for their passage. Now, they were helping load it again, this time with machines and sealed crates with warnings of fragility. The train would take them upstream faster and safer than the winding mountain roads that nobody else used any more. 

He had ridden the tram here to the river. To sit and chew some leaves, he had told his grandson. The tram had arrived on schedule, down to the second. The water seemed still, flowing without eddies.

Waterlilies crowded the banks, more than last year. One leaf became two, two became ten.
All the same. Maybe they liked the calm water.  On the opposite bank, the mine seemed still, too.

They had arrived in the city a week ago, but could afford a complete overhaul of their two cargo trailers with today’s profits alone. The cityfolk always bought the best items from the padded boxes and locked cases, paying with foundry coin. They had sold a few pails, too, surprisingly.

## Descendant

The descendant steps from his boat, the carved rings in his beard clinking. Each metal and engraving tells a story to anyone who can read it. Of battles witnessed, of armies marching and defeated, of empires collapsed, stagnated, or transcended.

The bow had scraped on a few stones when it hit the shore, but it’s quiet now. The water reflects something vast and broken-looking in the fog. A bell tolls in the distance. Once, almost like an accident.

He walks inland, calmly, without a destination.

A shape appears from the fog. A ruin. Remains of walls, a few layers of stone only. Cracked and worn, black scorch marks somewhere.

Something sticks from a crack in the wall. The descendant reaches down, and teases it out, carefully.

It’s a corner of paper, stiff, the torn edge blackened. Some writing is still visible.

‘…TRACTION WITHIN TOL…’

The descendant cannot read the runes. He carefully slips the paper within a leather case, all the same, to keep and to remember.

The river is supposed to have a bend here, according to the map he has traded from another crew. He can see the waterlilies, but no bend. 

The river remembers.

He does not know what it is remembering.

The ramp slammed down with a metallic clang, reverberating in the hangar. Kera felt the carrier rock back as it did, the hydraulics powerful even at their non-combat setting. Her suit swayed around her a little, held back by the restraints. A flicker in the corner of her eye caught her attention, and her head snapped around. Just the light from the door catching on Kahara’s helmet. Just the light.

Then they were filtering out of the crew bay. Kahara went first, Kera following half a pace behind him, in lockstep. Just like…before, or in training, more times than she could count. Then she was on the ramp, ringing underneath her soles, metal on metal. Her visor flashed amber, for a hollow space underneath detected by the seismics.

Kera felt her heartbeat picking up in response.

Now, she was on the ground – no, the floor. The plough stood next to her, the grey slab of metal and ceramics towering over her. The stomp of her feet seemed to bounce back from it, too loud. She veered left, Kahara right, her steps carrying her forward. Her shield arm twitched as she neared the edge. Nothing here, she told herself, they were back and–

Snap.

She was crouched before she realized she’d moved. Shield up. Whipping around, rifle rising. She stopped herself a fraction before it was fully aimed.

It took her a moment to parse what had happened, instant by instant. A sudden sound – a metallic clank. Her shield shooting up. Her hips pivoting her around. 

Ott was in front of her, his left hand rising. It moved slowly, palm to the front, thumb to the side. A gauntleted approximation of a fingers-spread gesture, half caution and half apology. His gun arm was down, the wrist-thick barrel of the underslung grenade launcher still brushing against the edge of the plough. The clank.

Her heartbeat thundered in her ears.

‘Woah.’ Kahara’s voice over the coms brought her back. ‘Remind me to never play catch-ball against Kera.’

‘Good shield alignment,’ came squad master Dalen’s voice. ‘Those extra hours clearly paid off.’

Kera muttered an apology over the radio. She forced her arms down, and straightened her knees and hips.

The pressure in her chest was back again.

‘A little close to a safety incident, though.’ Dalen continued, this time over a private channel. ‘But it takes a while to come off the breach. You’ll learn.’

Kera clicked an acknowledgement over the radio. He was right, though. What if she hadn’t stopped herself in time? A shudder went through her, from her neck down. She took a breath to steady herself, and then nodded. She would go see the advisor after the morning bell.

She took a few more steps, careful now, walking to the side of the carrier.

She stopped and lowered herself on one knee, resting her shield on the floor. Her rifle went on top of it. _13-mil bore. 15-grain smokeless charge._ She never left it on the ground or dirt, and never more than a pace away from her. She’d learned that lesson during her first week.

Her leg actuators hissed as she straightened, a high-pitch vibration more sensed than heard. She reached up with her now-free hands, unclamping the two seals on her gorget. The helmet scraped at her cheeks as she pulled it off. A few strands of hair fell on her face, lank and wet. The air felt cool.

Light changed. She blinked at the brightness. The world seemed more immediate now, but more…unreal at the same time, somehow. She could see wider, and make out more details, but they all seemed flat and meaningless. They didn’t threaten, or move in support. They just _were_.

Next to her, the carrier was ticking as it cooled. She could feel the exhaust grate radiating heat on her face. The air rippled above it. The vehicle stank of incomplete combustion, and of gunpowder smoke.

The surface was cratered with impacts, dots the size of a thumb-tip with cracks radiating outwards. There was one fist-sided crater higher up, blackened at the edges. She knew the impact. The carrier had pitched violently, banging her head against the wall.

Kera felt her stomach tightening.

She half-closed her eyes and took a deep breath, turning the helmet around in her hands. The surface was covered in dust and debris. A piece of brick was lodged next to the thermal imaging mount. She would have to tease it out during maintenance, and check the mount afterwards.

She angled the helmet so that her face reflected in the visor. The strands of hair were still clinging to her face, the color of a dusty road. The same as her eyes.

The squad master called. They were moving to the armory to clean and check in their guns. Then, it would be armor maintenance.

Kera took her helmet in her left gauntlet, running her right across her face and combing up the loose hair. Her fingertips could faintly feel the strands moving, the feeling transmitted by the sub-mil rods perforating the steel surface. She rested her forehead against the rubberized surface of her palm for a second, closing her eyes. Her mind strayed back to her enlistment, to her pen scraping on the forms. She could have been an engineer instead.

She let her arm drop down, adjusting for the minuscule change in suit balance without noticing. Her forehead felt raw.

***

The sun was setting when they gathered in the hangar again. She had her maintenance uniform on, dark green with old stains from oil and grease. There were a few lighter batches from ceramic dust. Everyone had.

It was her first time to etch the mission tally on the glacis of the carrier. The nineteenth. She’d make two symbols of lines and dots, the date and coordinates of the breach. It was an old maker’s mark system, Kahara had explained to her earlier. The marks were space-efficient, and could be etched or engraved in any smithy.

Kera had spent an hour just memorizing the symbols earlier that week. Since then, she had practiced them with pen on paper, a stick on sand, a nail on wood. She could make the marks with her eyes closed now.

Aest had fetched a wooden ladder for them to climb on top of the carrier. He set it against the track cover, shaking it a little to make sure it was secure. Satisfied, he nodded at Kera to climb.

Kera nodded back her thanks and climbed the first two rungs. It brought her face level with the fist-sized crater. Cracks radiated from it in a starburst of broken ceramics. 

Up close, it looked much deeper than earlier. She figured she wouldn’t be able to reach the bottom with her extended fingers. Two jagged rings of metal caught the light in the cavity, between layers of crumbled ceramic.

Kera turned her eyes. The knuckles of her gun-hand were white on the rung.

‘You afraid of heights, now?’ Ott called from below. Somebody chuckled. 

‘She doesn’t have a solid-bone head like you,’ Kahara launched back. 

Kera found herself chuckling and shaking her head a little. She continued her way up.

***

One by one, the squad followed her, gathering on top of the carrier. Dalen began lighting the small brazier, blowing at the charcoal to make the fire catch. The smell of woodsmoke began to mix with the oil and burnt fuel of the vehicle.

The brazier would soon heat up the etching compound to working temperature. Kahara could have taught them every small bit of its history, Kera was sure. Best to make sure he never got the excuse.

The frontal third of the carrier roof was sloped. It was slightly uncomfortable under her feet, but she could still walk to the upper edge of the glacis. There, she had to get on all fours to proceed, and finally crawl a bit on her belly to actually reach the row of tally-marks without rolling over and falling down. She’d have to etch them upside down, she realized, biting down a curse. She hadn’t practiced _that_.

Kera inched the last span forward. Ott was now supporting her from her ankles so that she wouldn’t slide off. She could just reach the row of tally symbols, feeling the roughened metal under her fingertips. Somebody handed the etching pen into her outstretched hand. The master, probably. The pen felt hot to the touch, the heat from the compound inside carrying through the insulation.

The squad fell silent somewhere behind and above her.

Kera visualized the symbols in her head, trying to translate the image into where she should first place the tip. The first dot would go there, and the first line would follow it, parallel to the glacis. She went through her reasoning twice, and practiced the pattern by tracing her finger against the metal. Deciding she wouldn’t get any more sure than that, she began.

The pen only made a faint scraping sound on the surface. If she craned her neck, she could just see her hands working. She wanted to, to make sure she deposited enough of the oozing compound to leave a decent mark, but not so much that it spilled.

The compound started fuming on the metal, a slight grey haze forming over the marks she’d already made. The smell was acrid and the smoke made her eyes water. For a heartbeat she was tempted to work faster, but quickly decided against it. She didn’t want to miss a line.

Kera made the last dot, lifting the pen off the metal. She brought her hands under her shoulders, pushing up and craning even further to see better. The marks looked alright to her. She had misjudged a gap there, but it wasn’t like she could fix it now.

She lowered herself back to the surface, neck and arms threatening to cramp. The position was awkward, but she would have to wait a little longer for the marks to bite, before scraping the then-solid compound off.

She rolled her head around to work her neck and shoulders. The glacis had pockmarks and gouges spread over the surface. She had been so focused on the etching that she hadn’t noticed them before. There wasn’t anything else to do right now, either.

The craters were almost oval-shaped, with a tapering tail. The impact itself, and then the plate bouncing the remains of the projectile off.

Some were deeper than the length of her thumb.

Her thoughts strayed back to the damage on the side armor. The fist-sized crater she had passed on her way up.

So…deep. It looked like it had almost penetrated.

It would have gone straight through Ott if it had. Thrown him across the crew bay, his body landing wrong. The overpressure punching into her chest and stomach.

Her teeth creaked.

She would have made them pay for that. She would have–

‘You alright?’ Kahara’s voice was suddenly next to her. 

Her fist was clenching the etching tool. The tip was shaking. The brushed steel body showed a distorted reflection of her face. She could see the scar on her cheek, from the explosion that had spalled–

A bell tolled. The hangar clock marking half-past. Probably.

Kera blinked. Just the hair clinging to her face again.

‘Kera? If you _are_ afraid of heights, I can finish here,’ Kahara continued, faster now. ‘Doesn’t matter. It’s not like we are jumping roofs while suited.’ A chuckle.

She had stiffened at some point. Her mouth felt dry, her tongue sticking to the roof.

‘I’m fine,’ she replied, after swallowing. She managed a weak smile. ‘Thanks.’

‘You sure?’ Kahara continued, raising an eyebrow. Then he continued,
‘Nice work on the dots,’ nodding at the settling compound. ‘I never get mine round.’

He eyed the etching. Then Kera, expectantly. 

Oh.

The compound had hardened enough.

‘Chisel,’ she called, reaching out with her hand.

Kahara stayed a moment and then started working his way back up. Kera began peeling off the mounds of compound, revealing the new marks underneath. She pushed up on her hands again, after a while, to assess her work. The dots were indeed round. Not tailed like the bullet scars.

She continued her work, easing the chisel a fraction under each mound, then turning it to peel off a chip. Push and peel, again and again.

The scrape of the chisel slowly hid any other sound.

***

The brazier had gone cold when she was finished and back on her feet, the back of her neck aching and trembling from the effort. The scent of woodsmoke still  lingered in the air. Master Dalen had come to check her work, getting on one knee on the glacis and then nodding once.

Kera was the first to climb down the ladder. Kahara was supporting the rails from the above, saying he’d felt them slip on his way up. She passed the crater on the carrier’s side without stopping.

‘Evening bell in twenty,’ Dalen announced, stepping down from the ladder after her. ‘Dismissed until then.’ He met her eyes and nodded briefly, his eyes softening slightly.

Kera watched him go. The hangar doors were open a fraction, too narrow to fit through in her suit, even sideways. The light filtering in made a blueish column on the floor, every pebble casting an arm-long shadow behind it. The sun was below the horizon already.

The rest of the squad gradually followed him, in ones and twos without hurry. Aest returned the ladder to its chalk-outlined spot on the rear wall, then waved a goodbye to Kera. Or maybe it was a greeting, a talk to you later at the tavern. She wasn’t sure.

Kahara was the last to leave. ‘You coming?’ he asked, raising his eyebrow a little. His forehead barely reached her eyes.

‘Go ahead, I’ll catch you later.’

‘You sure? We still have…almost eighteen minutes,’ he said, glancing at his watch. ‘Enough for a nap. Or snack.’

Kera found herself smiling. She liked Kahara. He made her time in the breachers almost enjoyable. They all did.

‘I wish I had your talent for sleeping. Go. I’ll join you soon.’

Kahara shrugged, smiling. ‘Your loss. More sleep for me.’

Kera remained standing even after he had disappeared through the door. The beam of light on the floor was almost gone now. The shadows were wider, and softer around the edges. The hangar was quiet, apart from occasional pings from the sheets cooling down. The walls creaked when a gust of wind rushing down from the mountains pushed against them.

She decided to walk around the carrier once more. She didn’t really know why – she wouldn’t see the etching from the floor level in any case. It just felt like a thing to do. The vehicle seemed inert now, its boxy shape blending with the shadows. It no longer ticked, and the angry heat had gone. The craters and gouges were still visible, but the edges seemed smoother in the dim light.

Kera checked that a toolbox hatch was properly locked, and then rested her hand against the rear track guard. The surface felt rough to the touch.

It was scarred, but it worked.

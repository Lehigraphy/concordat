He was standing in rank, legs shaky, head swimming and stomach churning. The _alarm_ had come before sunrise, though even he had realized it was yet another drill. Third squad, his squad, had stumbled out, and now they were standing in attention on the yards before their quarters. They were wearing their jumpsuits, and shivering in the morning drizzle.

And he had the worst hangover of his life. His _second_ hangover. He had had his first a month ago, from his coming-of-age ceremony, and that had been just a headache. This was a hundred times worse.

Then, the squad master arrived, barking a terse command for the squad to follow, and then set off running towards the mountain. _Ancestors save me._

He had dreamed of being a breacher since his first apprenticeship, and enlisted as soon as he was legally able. During the apprenticeship — basic training — he'd been worried they'd shuttle him off as a combat engineer or something. His entire family had always been Anvil. When the results finally came, his heart had leapt.

It had been a late afternoon when he finally arrived to the base, got all the forms signed, and found the third squad of third company - his. They had just been about to unsuit, helms off, looking every nut and bolt like the promos. The armor had looked even bigger and scarier up close.

Then his squad master suddenly had appeared in front of him. He hadn't heard a thing. Afterwards, he realized his mouth had probably fallen open, before he composed himself and made his report, staring upward. His eyes had barely reached the lower ridge of the master's gorget.

A squad master Kera, he knew, just from the roster at the time. Only a few years older than him, he'd thought, but surely a veteran of a hundred breaches already.

She hadn't even deigned to meet his eye, her eyes fixed at something above his shoulder, nodding to a blond-haired young man on her right. The man had introduced himself as Aest, his section overseer — SO — and taken things from there. He'd got basic gear signed for and had been shown to his bunk.

That night, his new squad had been silent around him. So, he'd asked them why breachers only had two sections, when everybody else in the Concordate forces had three. He'd thought it'd be a good way to break the ice. Instead, nobody had replied. He'd even caught his section’s third breacher — Kahara, he'd later learned — shaking his head with a weird smile on his face. The boy had felt his cheeks heat up.

Sleep had taken a long time to come.


He tripped on a root and stumbled on one knee, hurriedly getting up again. The mud had stained his shin a deep reddish brown. The shade brought back memories.


He had received his suit on his second day. It had looked like...pottery. Instead of the smooth dark-grey tiles of armor he'd the others wear, it had mishapen blocks of...brick. Discoloured reddish-orange, uneven edges. None of the pieces had looked correct.

The Anvil quartermaster had laughed and slapped his shoulder. He's probably seen his face, spare thought. You'll understand soon enough, the master had promised.

He had. In his first movement and familiarization drill in a mockup concrete house, he'd stumbled and crashed and banged every single surface of his suit on something. Against walls, against dummy furniture — he'd soon understood why they were built so sturdy. After the drills, he had been covered in red dust. Only a few of the brick not-armor tiles had been intact. At least the drill had been only with fellow novices from other squads and companies. 

That night, he had spent three hours making replacement tiles from clay. While the tiles were burned in the kiln and then cooled, he had worked on suit maintenance drills, instructed by the Anvil journeymen. Every novice was required to reach at least first-apprentice level on suit repair.

Since then, that had been his evening activity _every_ night after suited drills. The clay dried and cracked his fingers, but the lotion from the infirmary kept it tolerable at least.


'Keep up', the master barked. 'Including you, Spare.'


Officially, his rank was now a novice, but nobody called him that, let alone by his name. There had been an array of nicknames in the first few days. A strapling. A replacement part. A cargo. Finally, somebody had come up with a spare part, so now he was Spare.

After a week, he'd joined his squad in training, in his brickhouse armor. And his stick. _Breacher support gun, mock-up._ It was almost as tall as he was, and was surprisingly detailed with twin broomstick barrels and a plywood belt feed. But it was wooden.

During his first day of movement drills, he had cracked the barrels twice. The thing was huge and unwieldy, and he was still getting used to the forces his armor could excert. The first time, he’d whipped it against a doorframe. The second time, he'd bent it against his leg while crouching.

‘Don’t worry, boy’, Kahara had said, once. ‘Aest here got feed torn last month. Happens to everyone.’
‘You more than others’, Aest had replied.

And right now, Spare had finished vomiting in a bush. They'd ran up the mountain until they reached the bell there, and the squad master had kept a truly punishing space. The entire squad was leaning against their knees and sucking in air. All except her, of course. Spare had rang the bell and then staggered to the nearest batch of foliage to empty his...he didn't know what. They hadn't eaten breakfast, and he didn't dare thinking about what he'd ingested last night.

He staggered back to the others, wiping the corner of his mouth on his sleeve. For a second, he thought he’d caught the master looking at him, but she’d immediately turned her head away.

Then, she barked another command, and they set off back towards the base. The pace was perhaps marginally slower. It didn't feel any easier to Spare.

He thought he still tasted alcohol from last night, among the acid. Was that even possible? The taste brought him back to yesterday’s events.

The squad had been waiting for him when he got back from the armor workshop. Aest had told him to change into his formal uniform, that they were going out somewhere. Spare hadn’t been expecting that.

He remembered arriving at the tavern. It was early in the week, and the place had been half empty. They had sat down at an empty table in the corner, dimly lit by a lamp and the last strays for sunlight from a smoked-glass window. Spare had felt out of place, so he had focused on the table. It was heavy-grained wood, worn smooth from heavy use.

Then Kahara had arrived with a large tray full of small metal cups. Someone pushed one into his hand, and Aest immediately proposed a toast.

‘To Hammer’ he declared. Everybody emptied their cups, and Spare followed suit.

He had only ever drank beer, and some wine at a wedding, but this was some hard liquor. He choked and coughed, prompting everybody else to laugh. Somebody slapped his back.

He had barely recovered when a new cup was shoved into his hand and Aest toasted again.

‘To Anvil!’

Again, the cups had gone up. His eyes watered from the fumes, but he managed not to cough this time. Somebody cheered.

‘To Furnace!” he heard him go for the third time, louder now. Through tear-blurred eyes, he saw the cups go up for the third time. He reached for a cup himself this time, his fingers gripping it without spilling somehow.

Again, he gulped down the burning liquid, a little after the others had slammed their cups down. More coughing. Cheers.

Something changed. Kahara asked him how his maintenance and repair was coming along. Spare uttered some words, his voice slurring a little. Aest told that Kahara had still been cracking a dozen tiles a day, six weeks in. Everybody laughed, Kahara the loudest.

Maybe third squad wouldn’t be so bad after all.

Emboldened, he’d turned towards the squad master and asked if she’d been scared on her first breach. She’d visibly stiffened, her cup stopping halfway to her lips, her eyes again focusing on something.

Maybe that wasn’t a thing for novices and Spares to ask. He’d opened his mouth to apologize when Kahara had jumped up.

“First section!” Kahara called. Cups. That he could do. He was getting the cups now.

“Second section!” Oh yes.

“Third section!” Mission success.
 
After that, his memories were foggy. 

There had been a third round of cups by…perhaps Ott from the first section? Or was it even the squad master? And he had been…dancing? With whom? _By the forebears._ They had arrived back to base somehow, but he didn’t remember passing through the gatehouse at all.

They were back to the base now, too, standing in rank again.

The squad master didn’t even seem out of breath. _ Hadn’t she been out with them last night? _

‘Everybody go get some fluids, then suit up for malfunction drills. Practice grounds, fifteen minutes.’ she snapped.

Everybody groaned.

The morning went in a haze of headache, malaise, and exhaustion. Visor failure, his vision reduced to a slit. He’d cracked lots of tiles again. Knee actuator lock, limping. Position sensor misfire, his right turning every movement into staggered jolts.

The master had called a ten minute break, and he lay on his back on the ground, eyes closed. At least he hadn’t been sick again. Barely.

‘As you were, breachers. Novice.’ The voice startled him. A man with strands of grey in his clipped beard, the triple hammers of a company captain on his neck. Spare vaguely remembered the man wishing him and the other novices in on his first day.

He hadn’t heard the man come. The squad master would have, Spare was sure. 

‘You kids look like oxcrap today. You haven’t been drinking the Furnace, have you?’

‘Of course not, master. That would be against the procedure.’ Kahara said, from behind him.

‘That it would. Carry on.’

Spare waited a few moments after the man had gone. When nobody said anything, he swallowed a few times, and spoke.

‘What’s drinking the Furnace?’

Nobody said anything.

He drew breath to sigh when he heard a strangled noise. Maybe somebody else had almost been sick, too. _ Finally._

Then everybody burst out laughing. A real explosion of noise. 

Spare didn’t know if they were laughing at him, if he was supposed to be laughing with them. Somehow, he thought the latter.

Aest was the first one to recover enough to speak.
‘You’re not supposed to drink the third cup’, he wheezed. ‘The Furnace is the EW suite in the armor.’

‘And there is no third section!’, someone interjected.

‘No, it’s because Furnace is listening to us complain over the coms!”

“No, no, it’s–”

Spare lost track of the conversion. He found himself smiling, nonetheless.

The master was calling again. They’d be doing urban breaching next, alternating aspects, secondary hydraulics only.

Groans, again.

During the next hour, they breached the same fake village three or four times. His hands were still shaky, but he realized he didn’t feel like he would die any more. Only that he _might_ die.

They changed loadouts and positions each breach. Well, the others did. Spare was supposed to first learn his support role — his section’s Anvil aspect — properly. At least he had a real gun now, the two barrels tinted a slight blue in the sunlight.

For the last drill, the squad master was the shield bearer in Spare’s section. They were moving into the village —  walking, crouching, stopping again. The suit felt like tar around him, the actuators sluggish. The master advanced ahead of him.

She moved like a housecat, even in her impaired suit. A housecat that mashed right through a brick wall, but a cat nonetheless.
Afterwards, Spare stood panting, helmetless. His heart was thudding in his chest, and his head was being hammered. He was sucking in gulps of air through his mouth.

A shadow made Spare raise his head. The master was coming. He straightened himself, closing his mouth. The master was looking at something behind him.

And then.

Briefly.

She met his eyes.

A fraction of a second.

An near-imperceptible nod of the head.

‘Not bad, Zosef.’ She caught her breath. ‘A little quicker, next time.’

His head didn’t hurt any more.

Then, she was moving again.

‘Third squad, gather up. One more thing before the hall.’ That was vague.

The hangar was dark and gloomy after the sun. His helmet with its optics was still on his hip, and his eyes took a while to adjust.

A vague shape the size of a small building was standing near the rear wall. Must be a mobile repair and maintenance container for an assault carrier, he thought. Then he realized. He’d get to see the carrier now. It must be inside the container.

Then the lights came on with a clank of relays. And he saw it.

It was not a maintenance container for the carrier.

It _was_ the carrier.

The vehicle had to be as wide as a road. Any wider and it wouldn’t fit on a bridge.

They walked closer. The squad master’s — Kera’s — head didn’t reach the upper track cover.

Zosef reached the carrier. Up close, he saw that it was surrounded by a lattice of ceramic tiles. The tiles were dark grey and not brick red. The armor seemed a meter thick.

Kera appeared on his right. 
‘Embarkation drill.’ she said. ‘Let’s take it slow, otherwise Spare will be baking tiles until midnight.’ Chuckles.

He walked up the ramp after Aest. The inside was dimly lit by a red glow. There were six seats inside, with retraction mounts on the walls for the backpacks.

The insides were…tiny. He had to move sideways, and still scraped his pauldron against Ott.

They sit down, one by one, securing themselves to the seats. They were slightly staggered, Zosef realized. So that their knees and legs meshed between each other, like cogs on a gear.

The ramp rose with a whine of hydraulics. The sliver of electric light from the hangar crept thinner and thinner.

Then it slammed shut with a metallic toll, sealing them into the red gloom reflecting from the worn floor.

Kera spoke.

‘Welcome to the third squad.’

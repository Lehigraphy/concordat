Forge Master Paros had nodded goodbye to the last overseer… thirteen minutes ago, according to his pocket watch. The forge seemed larger now. The workers—apprentices and masters alike—had sweated and grunted all day to dismount the workbenches and dismantle the machines. Now, all that remained were dark rectangles where the floor had not been worn smooth by countless steps. Roughly fifty million of them since the panels were last replaced, by Paros’s count.

They had finished packing the components of the last lathe almost seventeen minutes ahead of schedule. Paros had decided—for once—to really flex his authority and send them home early on full pay. He had stayed behind, to sign some paperwork, he had told his overseers.

Paros rose from the worker’s stool, leaning on his cane, and checked the chalk-marked floorplan one last time. The wrought-iron tip of the cane rang against the panels—loud now, echoing from walls and ceiling, over and over, slowly blending into a fading pattern of hammer-taps.

They would begin installing the new honing machines there, under Crane Three, fed from DC-bus six. All three sizes of the standard hydraulic cylinders they would soon be producing would pass through it, so proper alignment and mounting rigidity were critical. Next to it, the gears for the pumps would be surface-finished. All was for the suits, of course.

Paros made a mental note to increase the spacing between the two stations by fifteen percent. Last-minute adjustments were not strictly according to procedure, but he trusted his experience. It would let the carts pass a few percent more efficiently. Perhaps even five.

Paros stopped at an outline on the floor. The workbench had stood here once. _His_ workbench, during his second apprenticeship. Hip actuator. Extension subsystem. He had cut and lathed and milled and polished it all—the cylinders, the pistons, the moment-transfer arm mount. Valves had been standard even then.

Mounting the secondary cylinder had always made his fingers ache, until his prototype J-wrench. Innovation from the Anvil had never been expected, but there had been applause—and even a goblet of imported wine from the Forge Master. The other apprentices had given him a ceremonial hammer, their names engraved along the haft. He still had it. Now, the J-wrench had served its purpose and would be replaced by something standardized.

They had been exoskeletons only—no plating. Everything tailor-made for the operator. The apprentices—Paros among them—had crowded around, measuring, nudging, questioning, laughing. He had calibrated for gait, for differences in femur length or hip-socket alignment. Learning.

During his apprenticeship, Paros had crafted three subsystems. Two for disaster relief overwatch, when rock slid or shale caved in. The third had been for caravan escort. The Master Fitter had even taken him along for one leg of the final trial, to verify the skeleton’s performance.

Paros remained standing a while.

The cane tapped when he made his way to the mosaic on the eastern wall. He had made the dam, painstakingly chipping each piece from coloured leftovers from the nearby glassworks. It was rough up close, but from further away it had been ... _good_. The work had taken months of breaks, and a few unpaid weekends too. Once, a section had fallen off - first, frustrated tears, then thundering laughter, like a pressure valve going off.  They had changed the plaster afterward, and there had been no more mishaps.

Paros took one more look at the dam, his chin dipping a fraction. He didn't think he could have made it now. Not with the way the days were measured.

A part of the mosaic would still be visible after they had installed the line for Pump Assembly, Size Two. The valley. 

Paros reached the loading doors. The foundations there had already been set. Next morning, the concrete would have hardened and they could begin unpacking and installing the new loading crane. They would need it to handle the throughput - an increase of over eleven hundred percent per man-hour and square-meter, amortized.

Paros started toward the exit gate. His cane made no sound at all on the gravel behind him.

The smith turned the misshapen lump in his hands. The metal felt…incorrect. The texture was different, and the edges caught his callouses in an unfamiliar way.

Before him, the fire crackled, the flames twisting and curling around each other, dying and then reappearing. It felt warm on his face. A gentle warm, soft, different from the near-white glow of his furnace.

‘It’s not iron,’ he said. ‘Doesn’t strike like iron.’

A log split, bursting sparks into the air. They rose with the smoke, like red stars against the dark, for a heartbeat.

‘How is it?’ asked the elder, a wool blanket around her shoulders.

‘It’s harder. Doesn’t bend when hot. Doesn’t break when cool.’

‘That doesn’t sound bad,’ the maker cut in. He was carving a figurine out of wood, his fingers always in motion.

The wind shifted, pulling smoke into the smith’s eyes. They stung, but no worse than in the smithy. It smelt like home and food and work.

‘Hard would be good for arrowheads. Punch through a horseman’s mail. Nothing would stop it,’ the maker went on, his carving seemingly forgotten for a moment. ‘And we could make ploughs that don’t bend when they find a rock. Or–’

‘I don’t know how to make anything out of it,’ the smith cut in, gently. ‘I don’t even know how I made it.’

‘Hmm.’ It was the elder, watching the fire.

The maker added a new log, stoking the fire, making the flames leap higher for a moment. The wooden palisade marking the edge of the village was visible for a moment.

‘What did you do, when you made it?’ she went on.

‘I didn’t do anything. I found it, in the furnace,’ the smith replied. ‘The boy must have dropped an ingot there.’

‘Put another ingot in the furnace, then,’ the maker said, the carving in his hand again. ‘The furnace makes it better.’

‘It’s not better,’ he answered. Then, lower, ‘I don’t know how to work it.’

‘You would learn.’ The elder, again. ‘But it may not have been the furnace,’ she continued, adjusting her blanket.

A rock cracked in the fire. More sparks flew.

‘It could have been the ingot. Or the charcoal, perhaps.’ She went on, the smith and the maker watching her. ‘We can change one. See what happens.’

‘Well maybe–’ the maker began, but a smile from the elder silenced him.

‘Tomorrow, we will send boys up-valley. Ask about the ingots they sent us. How they worked the ore. Where it came from.’

She paused for a second, and then continued.

‘The same with the river and the charcoal.’

The log had now caught fire, and the flames burned brighter.

‘Tomorrow, we do it again. Same furnace. Same coals. See if it happens again. Then a different ingot.’ 

‘Again.’

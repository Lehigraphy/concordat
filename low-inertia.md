_The gauntlet reached for him. Closer. Always closer. Something was leaking from the cracks, coal-black and viscous, running in streams around the metal. The fingers opened as it reached for his neck._

Rhel awoke with a start, spilling his coffee. He didn’t remember falling asleep.

He took a quick look around, right and then left. He was still alone in the control room.

Good.

Rhel let out a breath, closed his eyes, and massaged his temples with his palms. He didn’t remember the last time he’d slept well.

He looked out through the window. The glass gave a weird greenish tint to the view, and seemed to very slightly distort it at the edges. Outside, a team of technicians in green overalls was putting up a new pole. They worked without urgency, but without a pause. A man in a dark coat was gesturing instructions, occasionally handing a hammer or a clamping tool. One of them.

They’d felled the pole last night, after shorting the transformer and blowing the fuse. There were brief fireworks of arcs and bangs as the cables shorted, and then silence and darkness after a breaker blew in the transformer station.

After the thing had crashed down, Rhel had walked to the breaker box. Kicked it.

Stomped it.

Smashed it, over and over until the casing was warped and chips of ceramics had spilled out.

It had felt…_good_.

It didn’t now. The new pole had a new breaker box.

Outside, a cloud passed in front of the sun, and Rhel saw himself reflected in the glass. Faintly, his outline warped.

He turned away.

**

After the shift ended, Rhel made his way to the town and then towards the hall and keep. The monthly council meeting was tonight, the cobblestone streets were busy, most people drifting in the same direction.

Rhel reached the hall. Two banners were hanging on both sides of the gate. On the right was the Karsik standard, browned with age, stitching faintly visible where holes and cuts had been repaired. On the left, the Conglomerate, pristine.

A plain sheet of paper had been stapled below, listing planned topics for the council meeting. There was no heraldry, and no seal of authority at the bottom.

The guard stood to attention when Rhel passed, the butt of his halberd clinking on the stone. Rhel nodded, grinding his jaw tight.

Inside, Rhel stopped by the tapestry. It was huge, easily ten meters wide, covering the entire wall.

A shield wall was grinding against the enemy, a tithe-formation, armed with mauls and cudgels. _Explosions shaking the ground, tossing bodies like pebbles._ Mounted knights flanked them, ready to force an opening and exploit every breakthrough. The enemy were  some savages from the myths, dressed in mail and carrying banners of falcons and horses, curved swords in hand. _Cracked metal fingers reaching for his neck._

‘Rather splendid, is it not?’

Rhel snapped his head around. It was the old knight, standing with his head high and shoulders squared. The scarring was still clearly visible on his face. Something had exploded against his tank, Rhel knew, failing to break it open but sending pieces of metal flying into him.

‘I heard there were some issues with the new power line, correct?’ he continued, not waiting for an answer.

‘A pole fell, sire. They put on a new one this morning.’ Rhel answered.

‘Hear they’re bringing in an underground cable to replace the overhead sections.’ he continued, almost spitting out the words.

‘That sounds rather robust. No poles to fall.’ The knight answered.

A bell chimed before Rhel was able to answer. It sounded electric. The council was convening.

‘I will be seeing you at the bouting tomorrow, right?’ The knight added, raising his eyebrows and leaning forward a little. Rhel nodded his assent, and then they were on their way both.

The crowd shuffled into the main hall.

The lord was presiding. His eyes passed over Rhel without stopping, but then settled on the knight. The lord nodded, and Rhel heard the knight briefly snap to attention.

The bang of the ceremonial mace on the table announced the beginning of the meeting. The lord went through the formalities, and then handed over to a middle-aged woman in austere clothes.

The woman went through a list of plans, matter of fact. Upgrades to the mine and mill, arrival schedules of new farming equipment. A refinery would be set up next to the mine, and training for the personnel would be opened in two weeks. The murmurs rose at the mention of wages. Rhel felt a pressure building in his head again.

The knight was raising questions and objections, one after another. About a planned road intersecting traditional training grounds, and if Karsik festive holidays versus work schedules. The woman was making notes, telling that they would arrange the planned downtimes where possible, but that there were constraints coming from the processes themselves.

**

Rhel snapped awake, gasping for breath and heart hammering. It was dark, and the hands of the clock were glowing faint green. It was a quarter past four. He groaned.

The nightmare had been the same. He had been back in the trenches, half-deaf, hands trembling as he worked the bolt of his _familiar scorch-marked_ gun, over and over again. Nothing happened when he pulled the trigger. The iron monster kept walking towards him, inexorable.

Rhel shuddered.

When the monster reached him, he wasn’t in the trench anymore. He was standing on a floor, or perhaps on the ground. Still, the monster towered over him, impossibly large, filling his entire vision. It was cracked, and pitted with impact craters, leaking the black fluid. Heat bathed Rhel’s face, like standing too close to an open fireplace.

He always woke up when the gauntlet reached for him. That part always felt so...real.

Rhel walked to the sink, head heavy and feet leaden. He opened the tap, and leaned against the sink, breathing deep while the water cooled. He could still feel the heat on his face after three splashes.

He shaved in the broken mirror some time later. He didn’t know how long it took.

**

Rhel stopped at a cafe on his way to the powerplant. There were a few people in line before him, talking about the planned refinery. Rhel hoped he would find a quiet table hidden in a corner somewhere.

He ordered a coffee and a cheap roll of bread. The coins still felt unfamiliar in his hand when he paid.

Rhel sat down at a table, in the corner, partially hidden behind a wooden beam. He could still hear the discussion about the refinery, though.

The boy brought him his order. As always, nowadays. Rhel didn’t think he’d served the other customers yet. He set the tray on Rhel’s table, lifted off the cup and the plate in front of him, and even positioned a napkin on his left.

‘Can’t wait for tonight,’ he went, eager. ‘The bouting, I mean.’

A cup clinked somewhere nearby, sharp in the momentary quiet.

‘Could we try something new tonight? Like maces?’ he asked, while reaching to adjust the napkin. Rhel saw his hand tremble a little, from excitement. For a second, Rhel thought he saw something seeping from it.

Rhel almost told him to shut up, squeezing his leg with his fist below the table.

The boy didn’t seem to notice.

‘You were there the last time, and the one before.’

The owner called for the boy. _Good._

After he had gone, Rhel noticed he had spilled his coffee at some point. Good thing the boy had brought the napkin.

**

By evening, Rhel was in the bouting hall. The walls were timber, and lined with furs and paintings. The lord’s banner hung on the rear wall, surrounded by those of knights. He recognized the one hanging next to the lord’s.

Right now, he was on his back on the mat, panting, his dulled shortsword some distance away. The knight reached down, offering his hand. His left shoulder hurt from the knight’s cut, despite the gambeson.

‘You almost parried that one.’

Rhel was sure the knight had pulled the blow, by a lot. The mat had always been the place where commoners could meet lords and knights nominally as equals, but he had only tried it once or twice before.

At least they didn’t have maces.

Afterward, they met in the armory. The bouts were for show. 
_All of it was._

There was a muffled clank of metal on metal, from the practice hall behind the closed door.

‘No point hitting the line again. It’ll be useless in a few days anyway.’ Rhel told them.

‘It would hammer home the point, though,’ the lord said, stroking a few gray hairs off his forehead. ‘It would spare us the need for another, for a time.’

‘Demonstrations won’t drive them out of Karsik.’ Rhel snapped, knuckles white on a hilt. He was oiling a sword with a cloth. The edge reflected the turbine tattoo on his neck. Rhel turned it town before it reflected something else.

‘I’m not sure anything will drive them out of Karsik,’ the knight said, his tone conciliatory.

‘Not yet.’ the lord went on, in a low tone. ‘We need to be patient. I have received dispatches from Inner—’

‘But they can be fought.’ the boy cut in.

‘You fought them at Weiter,’ he continued before anybody got a chance to reply, gesturing at the knight. 

‘And you, here in Karsik!’ For a heartbeat, he was back in the trench, working the bolt again and again.

‘For all good it did,’ rumbled the knight. ‘Didn’t even reach their line before the tithe was smashed apart. Those damned breaching troops of them.’

He was brushing his fingers against his scars, eyes unfocused.

‘Got my steed mired in the confusion…lucky to make it out.’

Then his eyes focused on the boy.

‘There is a moment for a charge, aye. And moments for maneuver.’

Rhel nodded quietly, sharing a look with the boy.

The boy nodded back.

**

The next morning, Rhel was walking under the power line. The engineer in the dark coat was walking next to him, asking questions, making notes on his plate. A man-sized reel of cable was waiting by the transformer station, and Rhel could hear the sounds of earthworking machines being unloaded from trucks.

‘It’s good there were no further acts of vandalism,’ the man was explaining.

‘The Karsik grid is still low-inertia. A sudden drop in load could have desynchronized the generators. Blackout across the gorge.’ Then, almost apologetically, ‘But you know all this already.’

Rhel was thinking of the breaker box. How the casing had split, and bits of ceramics had spilled out.

**

That night, Rhel and the boy slipped through the darkness. The boy had broken two of the light bulbs earlier that night with a slingshot. It was new moon, and Rhel could barely see the boy moving a few meters to his side.

They reached the fence, and Rhel pulled out the cutters. They slipped from the chain on his first try. He tried again, carefully setting the jaws against a link. Its surface had cracks.

The link snapped, and the weight of the padlock pulled the chain down with a rattle. He should have asked the boy to support it. _Stupid._

They pulled open the gate, more carefully now. The faint screech of metal sounded impossibly loud in Rhel’s ears.

He tried telling himself he was being overly sensitive. There was no reason they would be guarding this place. It was a shed.

Yet, a part of him expected a monster to step out of the darkness, or a gauntlet to reach for him.

The door to the shed itself was unlocked, opening quietly on oiled hinges.

Rhel risked a small flick from his flashlight. He recoiled from the brightness, cutting it immediately. For a moment, he could see nothing, his heartbeat thundering in his ears.

Stupid. _Stupid._ He should have covered it with his shirt, and kept an eye closed.

The fertilizer was there.

Rhel whispered at the boy, to go tell the driver to approach. Should have used the lamp for that, he thought.

The boy sneaked away, leaving just Rhel and his heartbeat to stand in the dark.

He still couldn’t see much, and he jumped at every creak and rustle. Just rats and the structures settling, he kept telling himself.

In the dark, he raised his hand. For a moment, he thought he saw something running along it.

The lord had opposed the plan to the end. Said it was an unnecessary escalation.

In the end, he had relented. Still, he had insisted they agree on its use later.

Rhel had other ideas forming in his mind.

He would see how armor cracked.

And if it bled black.

They would burn his body with the others. 

Perhaps the city would live.

***

He had bought the medicine at the furthest reach on his journey. It had been a scorching city of white stone and high towers, of unfamiliar smells. He had drunk their tea and smoked their pipes, and he had bartered.

The price had been high. It had been staggeringly high, more than he had ever paid for anything. He had paid all the coin and gold he had, and promised to return with more. His word was his bond, and they had understood.

He had also understood them. A mixture that cured a plague was valuable beyond anything. He had agreed.

Something that valuable could not stay a secret forever. Either by coin or blade or poison, the ways to its making would get out.

So they had sold the secret while they could, and the trader had bought.

***

The trader had woken up to a muffled scream.

He had jumped up, still in his drawers and rushed out of his tent. The bottles on his table had reflected the light from the single candle.

At the doorway, he struck something hard and unyielding.

A flicker of movement.

Then he was on his back, cold spreading from his belly.

***

He had come across the first fever-ridden town far up-river. The trader had expected as much, the winter and the spring brought the diseases, and they spread fast in the densely-packed towns.

He had been too late, although he hadn’t known it at the time. The disease had already won.

He had sold the recipe, and he had sold a large part of his medicine, for a promise of future payment. A great payment.

He had been too late. The disease had spread too far, and the spotted fever had been slow to heal. When the fires had spread, there’d been too few left to fight them.

The town would never be able to pay him back in time.

***

The warrior pulled back his blade.

Around him, screams.

No trace of him would survive. They would make sure of it. Boats and caravans disappeared from time to time, but had the word spread, nobody would trade with the city ever again.

No word would spread.

Their bodies would be burned. Their rings and jewellery would be hammered flat and melted.

***

He had misjudged the town. His price had been steep, aye, but it had to be. He couldn’t afford another disaster like the fire.

He had been sure they’d come back with a number of their own that would cover his losses. Tear down some temple gold or something. 

***

The blade rose. For the final time.

He could see no pity in the helm. No hate. 

No soul.

Only his own reflection, stretched and blurred.

The city might live.

He raised his hand, fingers shaking and bloody.

His word–.

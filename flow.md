The water flew.

Under the bridges, around the rocks. Next to the streets around the buildings. Everywhere.

The spring sun was back, melting the snow from the mountains. It was filling the streams and turning the ground squelchy under a boot or a foot. Uphill of the foundry town, new streams had formed.

The kids had gathered to make the most of it. They had built a town out of, well, anything. A rock here was a foundry. These sticks here were the sawmill. A piece of bark, loaded with pinecones, was an ore barge floating downstream from the next valley. They had even built a soapworks there, from pieces of scrap iron.

The children were digging ditches to guide the flow through their city. Where the water slowed down, it spread wider, finding new ways for itself. Sometimes it found a new street to run next to, and the kids encouraged it by shoring the bank and digging the stream deeper. If it tried running through a building, they propped it up with mud and clay.

The kids were making a bedlam. And they were full of contradictions. One moment they were fighting, pushing each other around, stepping on the very buildings they were trying to protect from the water. The next, they rushed in to prop up a bank that had collapsed, on their knees together. The miner’s kids were building a delicate-looking bridge out of branches and chips, and the goldsmith’s girl was splashing away in a puddle.

And they were building bridges across the streams. They had dozens of streams, and they wanted the people of their city to get across them. He had tried telling them that it was a bad practice, that water and buildings didn’t mix, that the water ate away the foundations, but of course the kids hadn’t listened. They _wanted_ the river to go through the town. Already, the water had spilled on the town square, brown and muddy, but kids had simply dug a new set of ditches around it. 

He smiled. The kids had a mind of their own, and there were easily two dozen of them playing around their town. Many were barely off their mother’s milk. Almost as many were soon old enough to pick up a hammer or a plough.

Downstream of the city, a pool was forming on a flatter patch of terrain. The water spread wider, slowing down as it did. The mud and silt that the water carried were slowly sinking down, forming a soft layer on the bottom of the pool. The water that trickled over the pool seemed clear, almost clean.

Then a kid ran over, and gouged a deep groove in the middle of the pool with a branch. Immediately, the flow picked up, turning deep brown and opaque again.

The master of waste watched, and thought.

He remained watching long after the master of lessons had rung his bell, calling the kids to their morning lesson.

Downstream of the city, the pool was forming again, untended. It was again depositing silt on the bottom, prompting the pool to spread wider still.

**

‘The fumes have softened your head,’ the keeper of coin said, smiling but stern. ‘You want us to take on the costliest building effort ever because you saw some kids playing?’

They were sitting in the keep-hall, on benches around the ceremonial forge in the middle. The bellows were making a constant steady creak and huff-hiss. The forge breathed in tune, glowing brighter with every huff, dimmer with each hiss, like a warm heartbeat given form. The night wind was blowing already, so the bellows were worked by the small windmill next to the keep. Had it been early morning or a still week, the boys would have done it.

The master drew in breath to answer, in time with the bellows, but the keeper wasn’t finished.

‘I’m not saying the idea doesn’t have merit,’ she went on. ‘But you have been a voice for Anvil all your life. These sound like the words of a tinkerer, or surveyor of mountains.’

She took a look around, at the people gathered.

‘It would take years of work. Effort that could be spent on the fields, or making tools and nails to be shipped upstream. Taxes would be raised to pay the workers.’

‘Maybe I have indeed gotten old’, the master replied. ‘But the downstream waste ditches are overflowing again. Every spring now. Too much waste, and the people are getting sick–’

‘We could gather detritus to soak up the spills and prevent the smells,’ somebody cut in before he could finish.

‘The foul water would still seep into the ground and poison the people. It’s always those downstream who get sick first. Cleaning the surface wouldn’t–’ the doctor objected.

Several more voices followed, on top of another.

‘We don’t know that for sure. It could be the foul air, too, that’s–’

‘The numbers are consistently–’

A loud bang shut down the voices, at once.

The keeper of the hall had hit the anvil with her ornate hammer.

She waited a moment, then nodded at him to continue.

‘Digging them underground would be a colossal task, aye,’ he began, again. ‘But we could make them wider, and line them with brick. Covering them would–’

He heard someone draw in breath to interrupt again, but the keeper turned her head and raised her hammer, eyebrows raised. No interruptions came, this time.

After he was finished, the master of waste listened to the talks continue a long time. He was sitting close to the forge now, feeling the pulsing heat on his forehead. Sparks flew up from the coals, picked up by the draft. The bellows hissed, and the sparks slowed down, spinning around each other.

A line of iron tokens lined the brick foundations of the forge. One for each problem they had not just faced, but aligned upon a solution. There, an arc representing a bridge, its surface worn by generations of hands. There, a smooth block representing a block of soap, swaddles engraved on the surface. His uncle had brought the knowledge from the valley downstream. A scale and a weight. A watermill, older than even the bridge by its look.

A bang of the hammer brought him out of his thoughts. They were arguing if it would be better to separate the solid waste and then cart it off to the pits. Another bang.

Getting the waste out of the town was only a half of the problem, that was true. The master found his mind returning to the pool next to the scrapwork city. To the water slowing and spreading. To the pieces of debris spinning around each other as they sank down, like sparks reflected from the surface.

The sparks rose and faded. In the pool, their mirror sank and remained.

**

The water flew. On the streets, running between the cobblestones and curling around the corners of the paving. Then it disappeared.

The master of waste sat on his porch, watching. His shoulders ached, as they always did when it rained nowadays. He reached around, wrapping the blanket tighter around him.

The children were playing in the rain. Some were watching pieces of bark being carried by the trickles. Ore barges, he mused.

There were more children than he remembered, especially older ones. They had discussed that in the hall, though a token was yet to be made. They would need to appoint another lesson-giver soon, to teach them the numbers and the letters. Maybe the weight-maker’s son could spare the time. He knew his number in and out.

A bunch of the kids were trying to dam the water, gathering sticks on the street, reinforcing it with their feet where needed. A pool was forming, the water rising. Just like it was rising in the covered pits downstream. Heavier waste would settle on the bottom, and clearer water would trickle out.

But here, the water found a way. It curled around toes and under the sticks, disappearing into the grates in the paving. Well, except near the seamstress’s house, he imagined. The channel there kept getting clogged.

The master thought he could hear it gurgling under the street. Probably just his imagination, he thought. His hearing wasn’t what it used to be, and the kids were making a racket. It almost even hid the bang of the raindrops on the tiles above his head.

It smelled of…nothing, really. Just rain, now. It had been dry and warm for a fortnight, and the town had begun to stink again. Not like it had, in the past, just a lingering unpleasantness near the grates. But there _had_ been a smell.

Not enough flow, he mused. There was still more for him to do.

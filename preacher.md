The young man was fulminating on his box, in the middle of the central square. The Mountain would rumble and shake, burying the defilers and all those who had debased themselves with them under untold tonnes of rock.

Sighing, the Preacher half expected the man to start foaming at the mouth any moment. The Mountain sent the rocks that crushed bodies and broke homes, true. But did it not also send down the waters every spring, filling wells and making the soil ready for sowing?

His young colleague, now as good as screaming, tended to conveniently forget that part. He had been fighting the changes tooth and nail, ever since the beginning. At first, the preacher had been worried that he would join the sculpted martyrs lining the temple. But, the men and women with the black suits had simply watched, making notes on the slates they carried. The preacher had not seen them for months now.

The preacher turned around, his steps taking him towards the temple. It was his turn at the soup line, and heating the large kettle took hours. Even if they only needed a quarter nowadays.

***

Qvell came to get soup, like every day. He smelled of alcohol. _Different_ alcohol, not like the poison distilled in the old warehouse. Like the stuff he cleaned his hands with when he visited the hospital.

Qvell told the preacher to go fornicate his Mountain - like every day - and laughed. The preacher blessed him and wished him a good day. As always.

_The Mountain wouldn't mind._ The preacher chuckled at the casually sacrilegious thought. In his youth, he had preached in the other valleys, speaking to the bishops and holy women there, learning of the gods and spirits they served. By comparison, the Mountain seemed remarkably indifferent to what people did in their bedchambers. He wondered if the other valleys had young men screaming on boxes, too.

***

The temple felt empty that evening, a dozen people listening to his sermon. The preacher's voice seemed to echo longer than usual. 

His _voice_. 

He had preached since he was a boy. The words had come naturally - almost on their own. When rock fell, he would ring the bells of mourning and speak of mourning, remembrance, and acceptance. When spring came, he would ring the bells of joy and speak of growth and the harvest to come. Today, the words rang hollow from the walls.

***

The preacher remained sitting on the temple steps long after the last parishioner had gone. A bell rang. Its tone sounded different to him, somehow thin. End of shift.

He really should go inside, the preacher thought, to find some pen and paper. Tomorrow, he would be blessing the new smeltery. The..._bracing lattice_ would stop the rockslides. 

Perhaps the Mountain wouldn't mind that either.

Maybe he could come up with some new words.

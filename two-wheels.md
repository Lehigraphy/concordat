It was almost dawn, the sky behind the mountain turning from dark grey to a faint blue. Taren was inching forward on his bike, a leisurely pace barely faster than a brisk walk. The night had been freezing, and frozen leaves crunched under his tires. The turbine was whining lightly, pushing just enough power to keep the battery charged. The sound would only carry two hundred meters or so in this foliage, Taren knew.

The forest was waking up, he could hear. The morning birds were chirping and chattering. Somewhere near, a squirrel was barking angrily, scurrying up a trunk. A little further away, a stream was splashing gently. His suits' audio pickups were amazing in the relative quiet, Taren thought.

He snaked his bike between a tree and a boulder. The handlebars barely fit in the gap. The right handle brushed against the tree, making a little scratching sound, flakes of bark spinning gently down in his vision.

_His bike._

Branik and Zora had been going through different concepts for hours. A four-wheeler ATV. Three-wheelers. A track-and-wheel. Frame steering. Their words had turned clipped, and Taren’s head had turned foggy. The constant rattle and hammering from the workshop hadn’t helped.

He had cleared his throat, clearing it from the airborne dust. The two had turned towards him, Zora’s eyes making him feel like an apprentice again.

‘Two’, he had uttered. ‘Could it have two?’


The signal came. A coded radio burst from the overseer drone, somewhere in the still-dark sky. His visor lit up with symbols.

A mortar position, three tubes, its position triangulated by the drones.

The thumps reached him a few seconds later. Two kilometers, the visor told.

Taren flicked his fingers, sending another coded burst to the two other bikes of his section.

The bike swayed gently when the front wheel climbed over a large root. _Two wheels._


The order had arrived out of the blue one morning four months ago. Summons to the regiment overseer — he had thought he had screwed up massively somewhere. Then, orders assigning him to some kind of a development project.

That had made precisely no sense. He had been a breacher for almost fifteen years, squad master the last ten. It was what he was good at, and the only thing he was good at. The overseer had spoken something about a _latent Furnace/intelligence aspect_. 

Taren hadn’t understood. If requesting a drone reroute meant spotting an autocannon nest behind a building, his boys and girls would live. Of course he’d do it, but it didn’t make him Furnace.

Taren had saluted, the sheet of orders under his arm.

He had arrived at the workshop at noon, finding his way around the unfamiliar machines to the overseer’s office. An engineer had already been there, with four Anvils of a first master, and a late middle-aged woman in civilian clothing. He’d saluted the man, and offered his hand to the woman.

In less than five minutes, he had realized he should have saluted the woman first. Fortunately, she didn’t seem to mind his breach of protocol.

She —Zora— had explained the project, chalk on board, movements excited, the no-nonsense tones of somebody used to getting her way. A _mobility platform_ or something.

It had made even less sense to Taren than before. He was aware he was there to provide _operator perspective_, but being aware did not equal understanding.

But, he was used to not knowing and not understanding everything. The company master gave the command, and he breached. That was it, and that was enough.

In fifteen minutes, he had resigned to sitting still and answering questions when questions came. It would be boring, but he was used to boring.

Another fifteen minutes, and Zora and the engineer—Branik—were immersed in a technical debate. They needed this range, that traversability, those signature spectra.

To his surprise, he could piece together the idea beneath, despite all the technical and doctrinal claptrap. A vehicle for movement, probing, retreat, regrouping. A vehicle for a breacher.

It had been the evening bell when he’d finally opened his mouth, without Zora or Branik first asking him about weight distribution or fire arcs.

‘Could it have two?’

‘I used to ride bikes in the mountains, before Breachers. You get everywhere with a bike.’ He’d continued, before they could answer.

‘It would sink.’ Branik had shot back. ‘The surface pressure–’

‘Wide tires. Like off-track bikes.’

‘Hmm. Yes. But it would be heavy.’

Then Zora had cut in.

‘They will be suited. They can handle it.’ Then she’d turned to him.

‘That’s a good idea. You have another, you open your mouth.’ She’d smiled. ‘That’s an order, breacher.’


Branik had already been sketching the structure, brow furrowed and mouth working silently.


Zora and Branik had been discussing noise levels when Branik opened his mouth again. It had been an order, after all.


‘Turbine noise doesn’t carry far. Not in broken terrain.’ Then, apologetically. ‘Suits have turbines. We practice evasion.’


Again, Branik had scribbled something on the board. Zora had argued operational distances. Taren had corrected some ranges and suggested approach patterns.


It had been pitch-black when they had left. The workshop had been empty, tools neatly organized on the wall.

The next morning, Zora had already been there when Taren arrived, taking off a long dark brown overcoat. It was worn at the elbows, and the stitching had frayed at places. Underneath, she was wearing the same clothes as yesterday.


They’d begun building the first prototype the same morning. A few workbenches had been moved to make space, Branik issuing calm orders to sweating apprentices. Taren had helped sweep the dust from the floor. Zora had made a call, and a Hammer courier had delivered them four turbines from an autonomous ammo sled.


‘Better to have spares’ she’d explained.


When something didn’t exist, they’d make it. The main frame, turbine mount, rear wheel fork.


He didn’t begin to understand Branik’s craft, how he knew if this pipe here should be elliptical rather than round, or that beam two millimeters thicker. Sometimes, he’d draw something on his plate. Sometimes, he’d scribble on a corner of a paper, or simply stare at something for a few seconds and then nod.


Momentarily, the sight had brought him back to his second apprentice. Anvil. They had kicked him out, saying he had no patience for the work. Taren hadn’t disagreed.


Now, though…


They’d been discussing batteries, for silent approaches. Branik had gone on about pack envelope shapes, Zora about sourcing and assembly lines.


‘Suits have batteries.’ He’d said. ‘Can we use those?’


Again, chalk on board.


A step-through frame, easier to mount suited. Chalk.


Most of his ideas went nowhere, of course. Most of _anyone’s_ ideas went nowhere.


Taren had proposed mounting a mortar on the bike.


‘No. Can’t keep it supplied.’ Zora had cut in.


Branik had suggested, carefully, brushing his moustache, a coaxial gun. 


‘Can’t aim with a bike. Shakes too much.’ Taren had explained.


‘A turret then? On the center clamp.’


‘Suits can shoot single-handed. Shield-bearers do, all the time.’


Branik had been silent for a moment, and then sketched a mount on the handlebar, a breacher gun resting on it and the rider’s hip. Accessible, and doubling as an off-bore coaxial in a tight spot. _At least it might scare someone._


Zora had been adamant about the bike not having any armor. The mass would sacrifice range, she’d said, and it was not expected to stay around for a fight.


Taren had dug in his heels.


‘The bike dies, and we’ll be walking home–’


‘The suit has enough endurance–’


‘--walking home, with half a valley on our heels!’


‘The breacher has a point.’ Branik had ventured, combing his moustache with his fingers.


Zora had taken a tour around the workshop then, fuming silently.


‘Just the vitals.’ She’d said, after a round or two, smiling weakly.


‘Turbine-generator and the drivetrain.’ Branik had seemed relieved.


‘If we fill the tank with foam, any puncture will…’


Taren had found himself smiling on his way back that evening. The moon had been high, again. 

Taren had barely slept the night before the first test drive. Like a novice before his first breach, he’d laughed in the workshop.

The drive was batteries-only. Mounting the turbine had turned out more difficult than expected. Branik wanted it low inside the frame, but Taren had worried about mud splashing the intakes. They had considered a vertical orientation, but IR visibility and the vulnerability to top-attack submunitions had killed that idea. Last night, late again, they had decided to have the intakes on the sides and the exhaust under the frame.

Taren had carefully eased on the throttle. The bike had shot forward, the rear wheel screeching on the concrete yard of the workshop. He’d nearly crashed into a crate, but got it back in control just in time, hitting the brakes.

Branik had run to him, plugging his slate into a diagnostic socket on the bike. Muttering something about…ramps. It had taken Taren a moment to realize he was talking about how the throttle worked.

His second drive, a few minutes later, had been a beautiful circle around the yard. The suit had fought the lean for a moment, then given up and flowed with it.

‘A little resonance around the 1.8 krpm range, right?’ Branik had said, beaming.

Another silent stare. Then a nod.

‘Needs an extra millimeter, mid-mount.’ Magic, thought Taren.

They had done endless tests and modifications since, even rebuilding the entire frame twice. By now, Taren was comfortable suggesting new things. Once, he had even wire-sawed some plywood to demonstrate a support he had in mind. He had thought it looked hideous, but Branik had simply nodded.

After three months, they had brought in two more breachers as test riders for doctrine fine-tuning. A young man called Kest from Taren’s company, and a woman he had never met. Both had ridden bikes in their past lives. Apprentices had built two identical bikes for them, based on the latest design freeze, while the work continued on Taren’s. It was _his_ bike now.

Field exercises had taken Taren increasingly away from the workshop. They _were_ allowed to train on the valley bottom now, closer to the city, so it wasn’t that bad in the end. Taren remembered the hikes up-valley in his basic training, his heels aching in reminiscence. Afterward, they’d comb through the mission evaluations with Zora, and technical feedback with Branik.

Having a gun in his gauntlet again had felt reassuring to Taren. He could see that each artillery battery the bikes would force to relocate, every staging area they disrupted, would save breacher lives. And Anvil and Furnace too, he added.

Yet, he’d often find his mind returning to the workshop. A part of him missed the sound of chalk on board.

A low-hanging branch scraped against the bike. Taren’s heart jumped. He stopped for a second, listening. The mortars thumped again, six hundred meters away.

The birds were quiet now. A stream was still trickling somewhere. Another stream.

Taren set off again. The turbine mount was clicking softly as it cooled. He’d switched to batteries-only when he was twelve hundred meters out from the mortar position. _Suit batteries._

His visor updated the position of the other two bikes every twenty seconds. He didn’t like that, he would have preferred constant data. The entire doctrine depended on simultaneous convergence on the target. But, Zora had decided against it, each radio burst increasing chances of detection.

Four hundred meters.

Taren’s arms felt jittery, the way they always did before a breach.

The wheels were crushing the undergrowth beneath them, making a constant staccato of small cracks. They were too loud in Taren’s ears.

The mortars thumped. He could see the tubes now, glowing in his visor’s IR overlay.

Two hundred meters.

He flicked his fingers, sending a command burst. Constant position updates from now on. They could still break off, if someone was out of place.

According to the exercises and calculations.

All set. Charge.

He hit the throttle and reached down for the gun. The forestock was resting on the handlebar mount. Branik’s mount.

The bike flowed around trees and rocks, and he flowed with the bike.

The electric motor whined, lower than the turbine had.

One hundred and fifty meters. He raised the gun, single-handed like breachers did.

One hundred meters. The reticule kept jumping.

Seventy-five meters. He could see all three tubes now. Someone, a gunner perhaps, turned his head.

Taren fired. The crack of the gun felt deafening after the quiet, even through his helmet.

Earth and undergrowth puffed up, the explosive round hitting a few meters off the mortar.

More cracks. The other bikes.

Taren squeezed off another shot. Fifty meters now.

The mortar crew was startled. The loader jumped back, kicking on the bipod. The tube began toppling.
Two more shots, in quick succession. Splinters of wood exploded from the hits.

The mortar thumped one more time. Maybe the firing lanyard had caught on something. The round went whistling away, skimming the ground. A soft thud as it buried itself in the soil somewhere.

Taren was speeding away, heart hammering.

One hundred meters. 

Two hundred. Four.

The section came together again behind a ridge, a kilometer and a half away. Birds were singing again. There were no more thumbs.

Taren risked a brief status update, voice only. Kest was sure he’d holed the tube of his target with one of his rounds. Otherwise, there were no confirmed hits. Taren would have to speak to Branik about that. 

They set off towards the pass, turbines whining again. The bike was vibrating gently against Taren’s legs.

Ahead, the ramps would be dropping soon, he mused. He didn’t know anything officially, of course, but he knew breaching. 

They met one more time in the overseers office. The workshop was quiet. Peaceful, even. Crates had been dragged in, and multiple bike frames hung from the rear wall.

Zora had brought a bottle of wine and glasses. Taren mostly drank beer, or liquor after a bad insertion or when it was time to turn some soft-handed novice into a breacher. The bottle looked expensive, and the glasses were sheet-thin and clear. Not at all like the heavy green-tinted jugs at the tavern.

‘It’s good,’ he had ventured, carefully.

His bike stood next to the office, mud still on tires. Branik had run his fingers across the drivechain cover, nodding. The next day, apprentices would take it apart, measuring everything.

They sat in silence for a while. Taren swirled the wine in his glass, like he’d spied Zora do.

‘Aiming’s difficult.’ He admitted. Branik laughed.

‘Told you. I know the armor control master from ‘prentis. Have a word.’

‘You did.’ nodded Taren. ‘You did.’

A brief silence.

‘The mission was a success, though.’ Zora said. ‘No indirect on approach to Primary A.’

‘The bike did well. Got right on without them spotting us.’

He continued musing.

'Like the stratagem, too. Ride ahead, see what reacts.’

Then, a chuckle.

‘Shame we can’t make it fly.’

Branik burst out laughing, a deep rumble that made Taren jump a little in his chair. Zora looked thoughtful for a second, then smiled.

‘What should we call them, though?’ Taren went on. ‘Before the grunts decide.’

Branik and Zora looked at each other.

Then, at Taren.
